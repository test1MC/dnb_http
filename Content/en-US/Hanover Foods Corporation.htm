Hanover Foods makes foods hand over fist. The company makes more than 40 million cases of prepared food each year and boasts a vast portfolio of products. It makes and markets canned and frozen vegetables, canned and fresh soups, frozen entrees, fresh produce, soft pretzels, potato chips, desserts, and fresh deli foods, such as pasta and potato salad. Hanover Foods sells its products under the Aunt Kitty's, Bickel's, Myers, Hanover, BeanEssentials, Wege, Troyer Farms, Spring Glen Fresh Foods, and Sunsprout brand names. Nationwide, the company caters to several customer types, including those in the retail, foodservice, fresh home meal replacement, private label, military, club store, and industrial markets.

Hanover Foods maintains a headquarters office, canning plant, and frozen re-pack center in Hanover, Pennsylvania. Outside the state, the food firm operates a retail operation in New Jersey and a tomato plant in California.

The company has become one of the nation's largest fully-integrated independent food processors. As part of its business, Hanover Foods owns factory outlet stores for its Hanover, Spring Glen, and Bickel's brands in Pennsylvania and an outlet store for its Aunt Kitty's canned vegetable and soup products in New Jersey. More recently, Hanover Foods has extended its reach to California, where it operates a tomato plant, to make its tomato paste more efficiently.

Chasing after the popular snack foods niche, Hanover Foods acquired salty snacks maker Troyer Farms in 2010. It later merged Troyer into its Bickel's operations, adding the Troyer Farms, Dan-Dee, and Seyfert's brands to Hanover's snack lineup.

Hanover Foods serves several market segments. Besides the retail and foodservice sectors, the food company acts as a private labeler for other companies and caters to the fresh home-meal-replacement niche, as well as the military, club stores, and industrial venues.

The company was founded in 1924 by the Warehime family. Brothers John and Michael Warehime waged a protracted battled over Hanover's voting shares starting in 1994 when John, the company's largest shareholder, replaced family members with independent board directors and created a special stockholder class giving himself, his children, and the independent directors voting control of the company. Michael filed lawsuits contending that the majority of Hanover's common stockholders had no say in corporate decisions; however, a 2004 court decision ruled in favor of John. Following the court decision, John initiated a share offer to take the company private to avoid costs related to conforming to the Sarbanes-Oxley compliance law and Hanover took itself private in 2005.
