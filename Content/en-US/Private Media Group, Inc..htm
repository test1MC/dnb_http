The company opened its first Private Clothing (formerly Private Circle) fashion boutique on Los Angeles' chic Melrose Avenue. It also offered to acquire the defunct MP3 trading service Napster for $2.4 million. (The infamous online brand was eventually sold for $5 million to software firm Roxio, which later changed its name to Napster .) Milton became chairman that same year and was replaced as CEO by former Commerzbank executive Charles Prast. Milton returned as CEO in 2003 after Prast left the company.

Also in 2003 Private Media began offering a video-on-demand (VOD) service able to bring DVD-quality movies to computer desktops. It additionally began offering access to online content and commerce to cell phones and PDAs. Late in 2003, Private Media consolidated its broadcasting partnerships and began distributing its channels in the US. Shuttering Private Girls, it launched Private Fantasy the following year through an agreement with EchoStar.

In 2006 it joined with US-based Playboy Enterprises to merge the Private Gold and Spice Premium channels to form Private Spice, a channel operated by Playboy in Europe. The company also re-launched the Private Blue channel in the UK through a partnership with Portland Television Group.

Milton stepped down as CEO in 2009 (he remained as president and chairman) and was replaced by Ilan Bunimovitz who previously ran Private Media's online content division. Bunimovitz left the position after only a year and Milton returned as CEO.
