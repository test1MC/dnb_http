This news beast knows a thing or two about current events. The Newsweek/Daily Beast Company joint venture formed after the late stereo equipment magnate Sidney Harman purchased the ailing Newsweek from The Washington Post in 2010 and merged it with IAC/InterActive's online current events magazine Daily Beast in 2011. The Daily Beast launched in 2008 by former Vanity Fair and New Yorker editor Tina Brown. Beginning in January 2013, Newsweek ceased offering a print version and became an online-only magazine.

The marriage of the two publishers spoke to the future of print media (or lack thereof) in the digital era. In the wake of a deteriorating advertising climate that hit the magazine industry hard, Newsweek had a difficult time weathering the economic storm, and circulation dropped to 1.5 million in 2010 (down from 2.6 million in 2007).

IAC Chairman Barry Diller and Brown hoped the merger with Daily Beast would help Newsweek adapt to the digital era's rapid-paced news cycle, and later in 2011 a redesigned Newsweek debuted with a return to more photojournalism and informational graphics, as well as a focus on both young and old audiences.

However, the new design didn't pan out as expected, and Brown announced in October 2012 that the final print edition for Newsweek would be the December 2012 issue. Beginning in 2013, the title became an online-online publication, just like the Daily Beast . The company also reduced staff to adjust to the new format.

The magazine's previous re-design in 2009 eschewed the masses in favor of a smaller, more affluent audience. Rather than detailed reports of the week's events, Newsweek's content featured more columns, commentary, and long reporting pieces, as well as a higher price tag.

Newsweek was founded in 1933 by Thomas Martyn, a former editor at Time magazine who wanted to strike up some competition. In 1961 The Washington Post bought it. Some 50 years later, Harman, the billionaire founder of Harman International Industries , bought the money-losing Newsweek from The Washington Post for just $1 and assumed about $47 million in financial liabilities. One of several bidders, Harman was reportedly chosen because he pledged to keep the majority of the company's staff in place.

Months after he purchased Newsweek , Harman forged a new deal to merge the operations of Newsweek with those of the Daily Beast , which had been operating as part of Barry Diller's IAC/InterActive. Early the following year the two editorial staffs combined under the editorship of Brown. After Harman's death of complications from leukemia in 2011, his family retained its 50% stake in the company, while IAC owns the other half of the venture.
