At Hornell Brewing (which does business as Ferolito, Vultaggio & Sons) image is everything. Colorful, arty bottle and can designs distinguish the company's beverages, including AriZona iced teas, energy drinks, and fruit drinks, from the competition. Since 1971 the company has grown to become the top ready-to-drink tea in the US ahead of competitors Unilever 's Lipton, Coca-Cola 's Nestea, and Dr Pepper Snapple . Flavors include Green Tea with Ginseng, Pomegranate Green Tea, and Blueberry White Tea, among many others. Hornell Brewing's other products include energy and sports drinks, juice, and smoothies. Co-founders John Ferolito and Don Vultaggio own and run the company.

With roots in Brooklyn and based in Long Island, Hornell Brewing sells its beverages in the Caribbean, the South Pacific, Asia, Africa, the Middle East, Europe, and North, Central, and South America.

The company has seen the most growth in recent years in Germany, France, and the Netherlands, all due to increased demand for its AriZona teas. Hornell Brewing operates in Canada through a distribution partnership with Coca-Cola Canada.

Hornell Brewing and its AriZona beverages have become ubiquitous in the industry due to its global breadth. Nationally, the company sells its products in supermarkets, dollar stores, military commissaries, warehouse clubs, drug stores, restaurants, and health food retailers. Customers include AAFES , BI-LO , Costco , Dollar General , Fred Meyer , Jason's Deli , Kroger , Whole Foods , and Wal-Mart .

In international markets, Hornell Brewing serves consumers in Europe, the Caribbean, the Middle East, South America, Africa, Mexico, and the South Pacific and Asia.

Bucking industry trends established by big beverages companies PepsiCo and Coca-Cola has led to continued successes for the company. Its oversized 24-ounce bottles feature noteworthy designs that have inspired some fans to decorate their houses with them.

It also stays on top of beverage trends and offers flavors, such as white tea, pomegranate-flavored tea, and the high-in-antioxidant Yumberry (also known in its native China as yang mei berry).

Attending to the anti-corn-syrup, more natural food, and green trends, as well as tapping into the children's market, the company has introduced Arizona Kidz teas. They are made with decaf tea and cane sugar and come in smaller, easy-grip, recyclable bottles. The company has also made inroads into the organic beverage market by introducing flavored teas: Yumberry, Ginseng & Honey, and Pomergranate.

A class action lawsuit filed against the company -- alleging that the "all natural" labels on AriZona Iced Tea beverages are falsely labeled -- was dismissed in March 2013 by a US district court in San Francisco. The lawsuit was filed because despite the "all natural" label on AriZona Iced Tea beverages, the company's products include genetically modified ingredients such as high fructose corn syrup (HFCS) and citric acid.

John Ferolito and Don Vultaggio started delivering beer in an old VW bus in 1972 to some of New York's tougher neighborhoods. The wholesale business grew and so did the partners' ambition. In 1986 the pair launched Midnight Dragon, a malt liquor marketed with a poster featuring a scantily clad woman whose provocative utterance, "I could suck on this all night," offended Vultaggio's wife, among others.

The success of Snapple iced tea prompted the two "beer guys" to develop a competing product; AriZona Iced Tea debuted in 1992. Caseload sales soared fifteenfold within a year. Ferolito, Vultaggio & Sons won a First Amendment battle in 1993 to keep producing its Crazy Horse malt liquor after Congress banned it for being offensive to Native Americans. (It changed the name to Crazy Stallion in 2004.) In 1994 the tea products went into national distribution, and the company rejected a $400 million takeover overture from brewer G. Heileman.

Ferolito, Vultaggio found early distribution success by hitching rides on beer trucks, but switched to soft-drink distributors to secure stronger shelf placement. As tea sales fell, in 1996 Ferolito, Vultaggio unveiled a line of colas and root beer, but sales were flat and the line was dropped in 1997. In 1996 it debuted Mississippi Mud beer in bottles that resembled moonshine jugs. The firm introduced Blue Luna iced coffee in 1998, and the next year the company dipped into the snack business with Blue Luna Salsa 'n' Chips.

A new line of ready-to-drink teas, using the name of and developed in partnership with Celestial Seasonings, was rolled out in 2000. The company also launched AriZona Rx Elixirs (herbal teas) later that year.

In 2001 the company partnered with Allied Domecq (prior to its purchase by Pernod Ricard ), makers of the coffee-flavored liqueur Kahlua, in launching the non-alcoholic Kahlua Iced Coffee beverage. The company introduced the low-calorie Rx Total Trim Cocktail, aimed at dieters, in 2002. Flavored water and low-carbohydrate tea followed the next year.

In response to the growing popularity of ready-to-drink (RTD) coffees, in 2004 Ferolito, Vultaggio began selling its AriZona coffee drinks at retail outlets instead of just through vending machines. The following year, the company introduced SweetTea. The company billed it as an all-natural, real-brewed, Southern-style tea (think Scarlet O'Hara on the front portico of Tara on a sweltering summer day). In so doing it hoped to attract healty food cusotmers ("all-natural"), Southeners ("Southern-style"), and nostalgia buffs (SweetTea).

In 2006 the company began offering its green and lemon teas as concentrates -- a 89-ounce can of concentrate makes 64 ounces of tea. The concentrates were marketed to specific groups (campers, hikers, sport particpants of all kinds), as well as the general consumer.
