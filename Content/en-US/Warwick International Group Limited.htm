Warwick International has been cleaning up in the chemicals business for more than 30 years. Based in Wales, the company is a leading manufacturer of a bleach activator called Mykon that allows detergents to become more powerful more quickly and at lower temperatures. In addition to serving the household and industrial detergents industry, the group targets such industries as water treatment, biocides, textile processing, coatings, and personal care. In 2015 Close Brothers ' private equity unit sold Warwick to Berkshire Hathaway 's Lubrizol Corp .

The company's Mykon series of bleach activators are based on the organic compound tetraacetylethylenediamine (TAED), which is produced by the acetylation of ethylenediamine. The pure TAED is then granulated to customer's specifications for particle size and color. Is it the TAED that allows clothes to be washed at lower temperatures and is also used in dishwashing products. Acetic acid, a by-product of the manufacturing of TAED, is processed into sodium acetate and also supplied to the detergent industry.

Warwick is based in Holywell, Clwyd Wales and has a chemical manufacturing facility in Mostyn, North Wales. It sells its products across five continents and in more than 50 countries.

The company serves the detergent and biocides industries worldwide.

In 2008, Warwick's management, supported by Close Brothers ' private equity unit, acquired Warwick for $235 million from American aerospace company Sequa .

Warwick planned early on to expand through acquisitions, with a focus on specialty chemicals manufacturers and distributors in Europe and Asia. However, to focus on development of its manufacturing business, it sold its own distribution businesses to Dutch chemical distributing company IMCD Group in 2010. The next year the company widened its global reach by choosing the Moscow-based GEM Group as its distributor in Belarus, Russia, and Ukraine. Warwick Chemicals had inked a distribution agreement the previous year with the GEM Group for the Middle East and North Africa.

In late 2011 the company's main division, Warwick Chemicals, signed a contract with Rockwell Automation worth $1.9 million to replace the division's aging distributed control system (DCS) with Rockwell's PlantPAx process automation system. The new DCS is expected to reduce maintenace costs and provide better production insight.
