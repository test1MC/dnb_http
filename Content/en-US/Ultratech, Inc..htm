Ultratech's machines take the ultimate in high-tech baby steps. The company makes step-and-repeat photolithography systems -- called steppers -- that help manufacturers produce semiconductors, thin-film heads for disk drives, and micromachined components. Chip makers use the steppers in photolithography, a process during which device features are imprinted on semiconductor wafer surfaces through repeated exposures to patterns of light. The company's steppers expose a small section of the wafer, then "step" to an adjacent site to repeat the process. Ultratech was founded in 1979.

More than 90% of Ultratech's revenue comes from its products with the rest from services. The company gets a tiny amount of revenue, less than 1%, from licensing.

Ultratech maintains manufacturing plants in California and Singapore that also offer phone-based technical support 24 hours a day, seven days a week. In addition, the company has sales and support offices in China, Germany, Japan, the Philippines, South Korea, Taiwan, Thailand, and the US.

Manufacturers in Taiwan makes that country Ultratech's biggest geographic marketing, accounting for 44% of revenue. US manufacturers account for 19% and those in Japan, 9%.

The company uses a direct sales force. Semiconductor makers (including companies involved in chip packaging) continue to dominate its customer ranks, generating 84% of revenue, while 16% of sales come from firms in nanotechnology, which includes manufacturers of optical networking gear, thin-film head magnetic recording devices, laser diodes, and high-brightness light-emitting diodes (HBLEDs). In 2015, Ultratech's biggest customers were Siliconware Precision Industries , 21% of revenue, Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Co. , 13%, and Intel Corp. , 10%.

Ultratech revenue in 2015 was about $150 million, 1% lower than that recorded in 2014. Sales of the company's HBLED systems declined while ALD system sales increased and product sales overall were even. Services revenue, however, dropped 6% for the year.

The company lost $15 million in 2015, a better performance than the $19 million loss in 2014. Lower R&D and general and administrative expenses helped shrink the loss.

Cash flow turned positive in 2015, reaching $8 million from a negative cash flow of $6.6 million in 2014.

Ultratech partners with potential customers to develop new process technologies, which help the company sell equipment based on the technology to its partner. Once a customer makes a capital equipment purchasing decision, that company will generally rely on the equipment for a specific application and until it needs next-generation technology, making it difficult to sell a similar product to a manufacturer once the purchasing decision has been made.

The company continues to expand its manufacturing operations in Singapore. It qualifies for tax breaks for building in Singapore and will invest overseas earnings into overseas operations.

Ultratech sells a niche product rather than a broad line of semiconductor manufacturing equipment. The company has diversified into a few key markets including 3-D packaging and high-brightness LEDs to reduce its overall exposure to downturns in the chip industry and extend its market share once next-generation technologies go into production.
