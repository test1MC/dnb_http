CSR, originally Cambridge Silicon Radio, designs silicon radio chips. The fabless semiconductor company designs short-range radio chips for the Bluetooth personal network standard, which supports wireless connections between electronic devices. CSR also designs Wi-Fi chips used in wireless phones and other mobile devices. The company has added audio, video, imaging, and GPS capabilities to its portfolio and is focusing on the Auto Infotainment, Imaging, Voice & Music, Bluetooth Smart, and Indoors Location markets. CSR gets most of its sales from the Asia/Pacific region. Qualcomm acquired CSR for $2.4 billion in 2015.

CSR brought to Qualcomm a range of products that should accelerate Qualcomm's advance in markets for chips of the Internet of Things (or Internet of Everything as Qualcomm calls it) and automotive applications. Qualcomm also touted the customers in those markets that CSR brings along. CSR operates as a wholly owned subsidiary of Qualcomm.

CSR designs and develops semiconductor integrated circuits and software. It uses third-party independent manufacturing facilities to manufacture and test its products. The company's manufacturing is subcontracted to leading foundries and partners.

The company has manufacturing plants in China, Europe, Israel, Malaysia, Singapore, South Korea, and the US.

CSR markets to OEMs and ODMs through a direct sales force and (in North America) through sales representatives. It also markets through a global network of distributors.

The company's sales and marketing functions are anchored in Cambridge, UK and Sunnyvale, US. CSR has sales and liaison offices in Beijing, Shanghai and Shenzhen, China; Haifa, Israel; Hsinchu, Taipei, Taiwan; Seoul and Gumi, South Korea; Tokyo, Japan; Singapore; and Burlington and Plano, US.

CSR continues to enjoy a leading position in wireless audio supported by its continuing development and innovation to offer improved audio quality for end consumers.

In 2009, CSR merged with SiRF Technology Holdings in a stock-swap transaction valued at $136 million. The deal combined CSR's Bluetooth connectivity business with SiRF's Global Positioning System (GPS) location business to create a company positioning itself to capitalize on a large and growing multi-function consumer electronics market. Previous CSR shareholders own about 73% of the combined company. SiRF's headquarters in San Jose, California, became CSR's US headquarters.

CSR made another move in 2010 to improve its ability to address the need for increased functionality in mobile devices when it bought Belfast-based partner APT Licensing. The deal added digital audio signal compression technology to CSR's portfolio. APT specialized in streaming audio for consumer electronics such as cell phones. It had licensing agreements with Altec Lansing , Creative , and Sennheiser , among other makers of audio and computing products.
