RadiSys makes hardware and software that help its customers run their networks more efficiently. Its products help route video and audio traffic to systems that will process it most effectively, in terms of quality and cost. Radisys' offerings include application-specific systems, board-level modules, and chip-level components. The company also offers system integration, software, training, and repair services. RadiSys sells its products to companies that make telecommunications products, automated manufacturing devices, gaming machines, cars, medical instruments, and test and measurement tools. Customers range from Aastra and AT&T to Verint Systems and West Corp .

In early 2015, Radisys reconstituted its operations into a software segment and a hardware services and embedded products. The company had reported across four product groups. The change reflects the company increasing emphasis on its software products for networks. Under its new reporting structure, the company makes about 80% of its revenue from hardware and embedded products. The rest is from the software side.

Geographic Reach

The company has operations in the Americas, the Asia/Pacific, Europe, and India. North America accounted for 40% of sales in 2014 while the Asia/Pacific accounted for 38%. The EMEA region was 22% of the company's revenue..

RadiSys products are sold through a variety of channels, including direct sales, distributors, sales representatives, and system integrators.

For 2014 Nokia Siemens Networks was Radisys's biggest customer with 17% of sales. Collectively, Nokia Siemens Networks, Philips Healthcare , Arrow Electronics , and NEI Corporation accounted for 51% of the company's revenues.

RadiSys has not turned a profit since 2005, although its loss of $27.6 million in 2014 was better than its $49.4 million loss in 2013. Revenue in the hardware/embedded segment fell 19% in 2014 as network deployments by its customers slowed during the year compared to 2013. The software division was off 10% on softness in the videoconferencing market and delays in LTE small cell buildouts. The lower net loss for 2014 from 2013 came as the company had lower operating cost and tax expenses.

The company has refocused its efforts on providing software to manage networks for its customers and has expanded and introduced products for that market. Radisys' TDE-1000 suite, which leverages the company's FlowEngine software, is aimed at communication and content provider traffic management companies. The software package allows wire-speed packet classification and load balancing in support of efficient Network Function Virtualized network deployments.

To help companies better manage video and audio. Radisys is testing its MediaEngine software with customers. it supports of Voice over LTE (VoLTE) and WebRTC deployments. Nokia's OpenTAS will use just the MediaEngine product line.

RadiSys go its start as Radix Microsystems (later shortened to RadiSys) in 1987 by Glenford Myers, a former manager at Intel , and Intel colleagues David Budde and Robert Patterson (all three have since left the company). Myers modeled RadiSys after Intel, measuring worker productivity and linking it to objectives, giving employees stock options, and -- like Intel's chairman, Andy Grove -- leading classes on his company's corporate culture. In 1988 Tektronix , the world's #1 maker of oscilloscopes, bought an embedded computer (for test and measurement equipment) from RadiSys and subsequently invested $1.5 million in the company.
