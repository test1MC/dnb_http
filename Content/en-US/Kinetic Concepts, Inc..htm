Kinetic Concepts, Inc. (KCI) is moving ahead in the medical field by using the power of negative pressure to make a positive difference in wound healing. The company is a specialty medical device maker that develops negative pressure wound therapy systems, which "vacuum" a wound site through a dressing, drawing wound edges together and removing infectious materials. Its main set of products are the V.A.C. line, as well as ABThera and Prevena, which help manage the closure of open abdomen wounds and surgical incisions, respectively. KCI is a subsidiary of Acelity L.P. Inc.

KCI makes most of its sales in the US, where it markets and distributes its products through its own sales force. Internationally, KCI distributes its products directly and through local distributors in some 65 countries.

The company operates as the major part of the Advanced Wound Therapeutics (AWT) segment of parent Acelity .

KCI gears its selling to acute care hospitals, long-term care facilities, home health agencies, and wound care clinics in the US and abroad. Some of its largest customers include hospital group purchasing organizations. The company maintains an in-house team of specialists who help individual and home health customers negotiate paperwork related to Medicare and private insurance.

In 2014, the company revenues increased by 10.3% due to full year results of Systagenix (acquired in the fourth quarter of 2013), and increases from the expansion of products and certain markets outside the US, offset by lower rental revenues in established markets.

KCI is working to expand its presence outside of North America. To that end it is building up its distribution networks and launching products in new markets. AWT business is continuing to focus and invest in high-growth geographic segments such as Japan, Brazil, China, India, Turkey, South Africa, and the Middle East.

For better brand awareness, in 2014 Acelity rebranded KCI, LifeCell (sold to Allergan in early 2017), and Systagenix as a single company under the Acelity name.

Growing its distribution portfolio, in 2014 the company entered into a new distribution agreement with B. Braun Medical Inc. to be an exclusive US provider of the Prontosan Wound Irrigation Solution.

In 2013 KCI introduced the V.A.C. Via Negative Pressure Wound Therapy System, a single patient use, ultra-portable VAC therapy system that provides simplified wound care.

Growing its geographic profile, in 2013 the company acquired Systagenix, a UK-based provider of advanced wound care products, for $485 million.

As a doctor, James Leininger was disturbed to see patients survive intense physical trauma only to die from complications caused by immobility. In 1975 he and his wife bought a troubled hospital bed maker, renaming it Kinetic Concepts, Inc. (KCI). Leininger bought the rights to the RotoRest, which KCI began renting and selling. The firm's first decade was marked by slow, painful growth; its 1984 introduction of the KinAir bed boosted sales and put the company on firm fiscal ground. KCI went public in 1988.

In 1992 KCI sued its biggest rival Hillenbrand (now Hill-Rom Holdings ) for patent infringement on its rotating bed. Two years later Hillenbrand settled with KCI for $84.8 million. In 1995 KCI sued Hillenbrand again, this time filing an antitrust suit for more than $200 million.

KCI went on a binge in 1997, buying five medical equipment firms. Among them was Colorado's RIK Medical, which made non-electric beds that could be used in prisons and psychiatric wards. In 1998 KCI was taken private again when two investment concerns, Richard C. Blum & Associates and Fremont Partners, offered it a deal worth about $850 million. Legal woes also surfaced that year when the company was sued over injuries allegedly caused by one of its beds. In 1999 KCI formed Wound Works, a wound management joint venture, with Georgia company Coloplast.

KCI was acquired for nearly $5 billion by a group of private equity firms in 2011. The group included Apax Partners and two Canadian pension firms. The deal, which also included another $1.3 billion in assumed debt, made KCI a private entity.
