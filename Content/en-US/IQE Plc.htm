IQE puts your epitaxy IQ to the test. The company (formerly International Quantum Epitaxy) makes compound semiconductor wafers for customers whose specialized microchips carry out high-speed functions in communications devices, such as wireless phones and fiber-optic equipment. Compound semiconductors are made from a mix of elements and operate at higher speeds or with less power consumption than silicon-based semiconductors. Wireless accounts for most of IQE sales. Europe has typically comprised only about 10% of total sales, while more than half come from the US, with the rest coming from Asia Pacific. IQE formed in 1999 through the merger of Epitaxial Products International and Quantum Epitaxial Designs.

About 70% of IQE's revenue comes from its wireless products. Photonics products, which emit or detect light, account for 14% of revenye with infrared products accounting for 8% of revenue with CMOS++ products bringing in 1%. Licensing income supplies 7% of revenue.

IQE makes its own chips across its products line in factories in the UK, the US, and Asia.

Customers in the Americas account for 61% of revenue with the Asia/Pacific accounting for 24% of revenue with the rest coming from the Europe, Middle East, and Africa region.

IQE has two customers that each generate more than 10% of revenue.

IQE's revenue ticked up 2% in 2015 to £114 million. Increases of 45% in CMOS++ and 28% in Photonics revenue helped counter an 11% drop in Wireless products for the year. Sales also surged in Europe (up 126%) while sales in the Americas slipped 11%.

The company's 2015 profit of almost £20 million was 30% higher than 2014's net income. IQE combined higher sales with decreases in adjustments and expenses.

Cash flow increased to £19 million in 2015, 30% higher than in 2014.

IQE strengthened its arsenal of technology through a licensing agreement with Silex Systems Limited 's subsidiary, Translucent Inc., that brought Translucent's cREO technology. The technology enables the manufacture of a range of compound semiconductor on silicon products, including gallium nitride on silicon for the power switching and RF technologies markets.
