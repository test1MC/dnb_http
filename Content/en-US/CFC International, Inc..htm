At CFC International, beauty by design is only skin deep. The company makes transferable chemical coatings to beautify, protect, and add other kinds of functionality to consumer products. It makes complex functional coatings such as simulated metal used on cosmetic containers and simulated wood grain for ready-to-assemble furniture. CFC also makes holographic authentication seals (popular on tickets and software), heat transfer labels for pharmaceuticals, and magnetic strips for security and credit card uses. The company is a part of multi-industry conglomerate Illinois Tool Works .

CFC has warehouses and manufacturing facilities in the US (Illinois) and Europe (Germany).

The company makes and markets chemically complex multi-layer transferable coatings laminates and sophisticated holographic technologies which are not only decorative but also provide value-added functionality such as abrasion and chemical resistance.

It offers a wide range of products for security and transaction cards including HoloLam Plus (a full face holographic laminate), magnetic stripe, scratch-off foils, and signature panel.

Sales and Marketing

CFC's technologies are used in a range of end products, including automobile applications, battery cases, cattle tags, cosmetic containers, food and beverage containers, industrial safety signs, picture frames, and toys.

Founder Roger Hruby (who formed the company in 1988) had controlled about 60% of CFC before ITW acquired the company in late 2006 for about $75 million. CFC had previously hired investment firm Lincoln International to evaluate strategic opportunities. In mid-2005, private equity firm Audax Group agreed to pay $109 million to purchase the company; however, the deal was later terminated. CFC then agreed to be acquired by Quad-C Management for $75 million, a deal that was announced in early 2006. That deal was scuttled due to concerns over CFC's product quality. (In 2006 credit card issuer Visa advised its member institutions to stop using CFC's magnetic strips on its cards.)

Later in 2006, CFC made the deal to be acquired by Illinois Tool Works , a diversified manufacturing company that makes everything from nail guns to commercial kitchen appliances to electronics chemicals.
