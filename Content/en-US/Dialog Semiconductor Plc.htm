Dialog Semiconductor keeps all sorts of electronic devices talking to each other. The company develops mixed-signal (analog plus digital) chips used in wireless communications, audio, automotive, industrial, and other applications. Dialog, which outsources its manufacturing, specializes in audio codecs (coder/decoders) and power management chips for mobile phones, including smartphones. Mobile systems represent more than 80% of sales. Customers include Apple , Bosch , and Sony Ericsson . Customers in China account for about 80% of sales.

Dialog's Mobile Systems unit brings in 82% of its revenue from power management and audio chips. The company's Connectivity segment, 9% of revenue, makes short-range wireless, digital cordless, Bluetooth, and VoIP technologies. The segment accounted for 9% of total net sales in fiscal 2015. Products from its Power Conversion segment, 6% of revenue, include AC/DC adapters for portable devices and LED lighting products. For the automotive and industrial markets, 3% of revenue, Dialog makes chips for controlling electronic systems.

Dialog engages third-party semiconductor manufacturers to make its products.

From its base in Reading, UK, Dialog operates 31 locations -- sales and design -- in 14 countries. Customers in China account for about 80% of sales with Hong Kong customers generating 12% of sales.

Dialog's five biggest customers are Apple, Panasonic , Bosch, Gigaset , and Xiaomi and together they account for 85% of the company's revenue.

In 2015 Dialog's sales increased 17% to $1.3 billion from 2014, riding an 18% improvement of Mobile Systems revenue. Mobile had high volumes from power management and the increasing number of wearable devices on the market.

Net Income jumped 29% to about $179 million in 2015 from 2014 on the higher revenue.

In 2015 Dialog introduced a single chip for Bluetooth for wearable devices, which should help the company gain market share in the next generation of wearables.

The company developed a partnership that could help it dig deeper into the Chinese consumer market. Dialog teamed up with ShunSin Technology, a Foxconn subsidiary, and Dyna Image, a Lite-On subsidiary, to provide increased system content for smartphones, wearables, and smart digital lighting devices

The deal that Dialog had to buy Atmel , a maker of microcontrollers, fell through in early 2016. Microchip came in with a $3.6 billion offer, outstripping Dialog's bid. Dialog initially offered $4.6 billion for Atmel, but the value of the declined as Atmel's stock slid in the intervening months. Dialog is due a $137 million termination fee from Atmel.
