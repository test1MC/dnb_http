Rolls-Royce North America takes care of Rolls-Royce 's business in the northern hemisphere: More of the UK giant's aircraft engines, gas turbines, diesel engines, power generation sets, marine engines and propellers, and distribution systems are made in the US than any other country. The US is also Rolls-Royce's largest defense market. The North American arm heads up all regional activities, as well as the global business of Energy Systems and Helicopters, and others, Defense and LibertyWorks, Naval Marine, Engine Services, and Optimized Systems & Solutions. It has 12 facilities that handle R&D to design and engineering, manufacture and assembly, as well as test, repair, and overhaul of the Rolls-Royce lineup.

The company, which serves clients around the world, has 68 locations across the US and Canada. Rolls-Royce equipment is used by some 160 military aviation customers and 4,000 marine customers globally.

Rolls-Royce North America focuses on the Civil Aerospace, Defense Aerospace, Marine, and Energy markets.

In the civil aerospace business, Rolls-Royce North America is winning work building turboprop engines and turboshafts (gas turbines). The bulk of Rolls-Royce engines are ordered by American airframe makers such as Airbus , Boeing , Bombardier , and Raytheon . As significant, its engine management and maintenance program caters to commercial and regional jetliners, and business jets. In combination with the company's other aftermarket services, sales of the service package account for more than half of the company's annual revenues.

Rolls-Royce, however, concentrates most of its efforts in the defense market, and, to this end, headquarters the president of its defense in the US. Although the company's engine and related technologies are used in military aircraft and vessels around the globe, the US Department of Defense is its top government customer. Rolls-Royce fixed and rotary-wing aircraft are used by all branches of the US Armed Forces. Moreover, one-quarter of US military aircraft are powered by its engines. The company support repairs and overhauls of its engine portfolio at more than a dozen sites worldwide.

Rolls-Royce North America also taps commercial and naval marine markets, offering a slew of gas turbines and diesel engines for seafaring power requirements. Its lineup runs from propellers and tunnel thrusters to steering gear and stabilizers, water jets, bearings, and deck machinery. Rolls-Royce equipment has been installed on more than 30,000 vessels, including those of 70 navies.

The president of Rolls-Royce's energy business is also based in the US. The energy portfolio includes industrial gas turbine compression and power generation packages for on-and-offshore projects as well as pipeline applications. Its Trent 60 is touted as the market's most efficient and powerful aero-derived gas turbine.

Rolls-Royce North America, through its acquisitions and alliances, has moved from a national company to one with a worldwide presence, capable of serving nearly every market segment in aircraft and aircraft engines. The company's joint ventures include IAE International Aero Engines, which builds aircraft engines with Pratt & Whitney , and an arrangement with General Electric to build fighter engines, the F136 interchangeable engine for the F-35 Lightning II (also known as the Joint Strike Fighter). It partners via Light Helicopter Turbine Engine to manufacture helicopter engines with Honeywell , and with AMR subsidiary American Airlines to make, repair, and overhaul aircraft engines. Of its 600 airline customers, American Airlines is the largest. It also operates Rolls-Royce & Partners Finance along with GATX .

In 2013, the company's parent Rolls-Royce acquired PKMJ Technical Services, a 200-employee nuclear engineering services firm near Pittsburgh, accelerating the company's growth in the nuclear services market.

Growing its jet engine test operations, in 2012 Rolls-Royce North America added a second jet engine test stand at the NASA John C. Stennis Space Center in Hancock County, Mississippi.

Rolls-Royce opened its first US office in 1906.
