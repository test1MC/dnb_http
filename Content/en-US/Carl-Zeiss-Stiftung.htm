Carl-Zeiss-Stiftung, also known as the Carl-Zeiss Foundation, owns two independently operated businesses, Carl Zeiss and Schott AG . Carl Zeiss manufactures optical devices, such as camera and cinematography lenses -- for which it won two technical Academy Awards -- and microscopes, binoculars, and eyeglass lenses; it also produces optical systems for medical, astronomy, semiconductor and nanotechnology, and industrial applications. Schott makes glass for cooking surfaces, mirrors, optical fibers, solar energy, stemware, television screens, and pharmaceutical packaging, as well as hermetic enclosures to protect electronic components.

Carl Zeiss AG is represented with its own branches in more than 30 countries and total represented in more than 100 countries and SCHOTT AG has sales offices in more than 40 countries.

The Foundation was formed by German physicist, optometrist, and entrepreneur Ernst Abbe in 1889. Abbe, who along with Carl Zeiss and Otto Schott founded Carl Zeiss in 1846 and Schott in 1884, envisioned an engine that would drive research and development in the natural and engineering sciences, thereby enabling sustained, strategic development of Carl Zeiss and Schott. To this end, the foundation funds programs for budding scientists and university studies located in Baden-Wuerttemberg, Rhineland-Palatinate, and Thuringia, Germany. It receives dividend payments from both subsidiaries according to their respective financial strength.
