Alfa Laval, a subsidiary of Sweden-based Alfa Laval AB , makes separators (centrifuges), heat exchangers (evaporators and condensers), decanters, tank equipment, pumps, and valves under brand names Contherm, Sharples, Ketema, and Champ, among others. Alfa Laval's products are used in a host of industries, from dairy and food processing to petrochemicals and steel manufacturing. The company also offers spare parts, reconditioned heat exchangers, and round-the-clock equipment-monitoring systems. The company has about a dozen locations in the US, including four manufacturing plants, an international distribution center, and seven service and repair centers.

Alfa Laval´s products are sold in 100 countries; among that total, 50 have their own sales organizations. The company has 20 large production units (12 in Europe, six in Asia, and two in the US), and 70 service centers.

Alfa Laval´s operations are based on three technologies: heat transfer, separation, and fluid handling. It targets 10 customer segments, which are divided into two divisions: Process Technology and Equipment.

Alfa Laval's primary purpose is to build its parent's aftermarket business in North America, the largest aftermarket opportunity (next to Western Europe) due to its mature installed equipment base. The US subsidiary has fueled its growth by developing a portfolio of products and services, such as equipment repairs, maintenance, replacement components, upgrades, and modernization. The company uses acquisitions to add related products and open up sales channels, as well as to expand the company's presence in the North American market.

In 2013 Alfa Laval acquired the US-based company Air Cooled Exchangers, LLC (ACE), a maker of custom-engineered air-cooled heat exchangers that are used to cool air, natural gas, oil and water in the natural gas market as well as other energy-related end markets. The acquisition brought a new range of products to the company's heat transfer portfolio at a time when demand for air-cooled heat exchangers is increasing, especially in the natural gas industry.

Alfa Laval in 2012 also snapped up Gamajet Cleaning Systems, a provider of tank cleaning machines and self-contained and portable cleaning systems catering to the North American industrial and sanitary markets. Apart from expanding the company's product portfolio, the acquisition extended its reach to the industrial market. In the same year, Alfa Laval swallowed up Ashbrook Simon-Hartley, a provider of belt filter presses based in Houston. Ashbrook also has offices in the UK, Chile, and Brazil.

Besides acquisitions, Alfa Laval is expanding its presence in the US through major contracts. The company landed its largest deal ever in late 2010. The SEK 250 million (about $37 million) order for decanter centrifuges was made by a Chicago wastewater plant -- the largest sewage treatment plant in the world. Deliveries will continue through 2013.

Alfa Laval Inc. was formed in 2000 to bring all of the Swedish parent's US activities under one organizational roof. However, the business took root in the US as a sales company, DeLaval Separator Co., in 1886. In 1890 American Philip Sharples inked an agreement with Gustav DeLaval (the inventor of the centrifugal cream separator and the mechanical milking machine) allowing Sharples to use the Swedish technology to manufacture cream separators in the US. Sharples' factory was operational until 1990.
