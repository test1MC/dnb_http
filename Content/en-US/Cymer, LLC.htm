Cymer operates with a laser-light focus. The company is a top maker of excimer lasers, light sources used by chip makers to pattern advanced semiconductors. Its products let semiconductor manufacturers produce smaller, faster microchips. Cymer supplies deep-ultraviolet (DUV) and extreme-ultraviolet (EUV) light sources to lithography tool manufacturers, who integrate the light sources into wafer steppers and scanners supplied to chip makers. Cymer also provides replacement parts and support through OnPulse, with sales based on the number of pulses used by customers on the light sources covered under the program. In 2013 the company was taken private by ASML Holding .

Netherlands-based ASML, one of the world's leading makers of semiconductor manufacturing equipment, acquired Cymer in February 2013 for â‚¬1.95 billion (about $2.5 billion). The purchase will aid ASML in its pursuit of next-generation chips for smartphones and tablets.

Cymer has made most of the excimer laser systems now in use, and continues to introduce advances (such as systems that use krypton flouride instead of argon fluoride light sources) that are on the technological forefront. Its technological differentiation plays up the ever-shrinking features of semiconductors and the growing need for photolithography systems to operate within specific nanometer dimensions.

Most of its products are manufactured in the US, though refurbished replacement parts are produced in South Korea. The company also maintains customer support field offices located across China, Japan, the Netherlands, Singapore, South Korea, Taiwan, and the US.

Cymer has had a limited number of customers; four of them - ASML (prior to the acquisition), Nikon , SK Hynix, and Samsung Electronics - represented almost 60% of sales. Customers in the US represented about a third of sales.

Chip makers are looking for ways to extend current technologies as the circuits gets smaller and closer together. Cymer's technologies are a part of that effort. In 2014, Cymer increased the yield of silicon wafers made with EUV technology to 500 per day, meeting a target set by its customers. The 2015 productivity target is 1,000 wafers per day. In 2014, Cymer and ASML received orders for the fourth-generation NXE:3350B systems. Two previously delivered XE:3300B systems were to be upgraded to NXE:3350B level performance.

University of California at San Diego PhDs Robert Akins (chairman and CEO) and Richard Sandstrom (SVP and chief technology advisor) founded Cymer in 1986. Both previously worked for laser and defense system maker HLX (now part of General Dynamics ) and quit; they were so wary of the all-consuming high-tech culture that they seriously contemplated opening a Wendy's fast food restaurant together.

To save costs, the duo built their own laser from lathes and other equipment at their alma mater's machine shop. Akins and Sandstrom took out second mortgages on their homes before National Semiconductor president Charles Sporck, president of the SEMATECH semiconductor research consortium, expressed interest in their ideas.

In 1988 venture capitalist Richard Abraham, a former manager for Texas Instruments , invested in Cymer, stipulating that the company focus its efforts on semiconductor applications. Cymer shipped its first semiconductor photolithography product that year. Rivals Nikon and Canon , impressed with the technology, teamed to buy a minority stake (sold off by 1997).

The company received $2 million in funding from Mitsubishi Electric 's semiconductor group in 1991. The following year Cymer licensed its technology to Japanese electronics maker Seiko .

The company went public in 1996.
