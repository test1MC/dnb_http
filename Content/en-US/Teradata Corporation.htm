Trillions of bytes of data don't faze Teradata. The company designs and implements enterprise data warehousing systems that store and analyze information about customers, financials, and operations. Products include its core database software, hardware components, and applications for managing demand and supply chains, marketing, performance, and risk. It also offers consulting, support, and training services. Teradata's hardware products are assembled by Flextronics using components supplied by such vendors as NetApp and Intel . It serves some 2,600 customers in the financial services, government, health care, manufacturing, and transportation industries, among others; most of its sales are from the Americas.

About 42% of Teradata's revenue comes from the sale of its hardware and software with services accounting for the rest. Consulting services are about 31% of total revenue and maintenance services kick in 27%.

Teradata has about 120 facilities in some 75 countries across the globe. The US is the company's largest market, representing about 56% of revenue, with international sales accounting for the rest.

Teradata primarily uses a direct sales force to market its products, but it also sells through distributors and resellers. Strategic partnerships with global and regional systems integrators, independent software vendors, and consultants also help to extend its sales and marketing reach. Systems integration partners include Accenture , Cap Gemini , Deloitte , Cognizant , Wipro , and IBM Global Services .

The company has a widespread customer base with its top 10 clients accounting for just 14% of its revenue.

Teredata's revenue slipped 7% to $2.5 billion in 2015 from $2.7 billion in 2014. The company cited constrained corporate IT budgets, fewer orders from large customers, and the impact of foreign currency exchange for the revenue reduction.

The company reported a $214 million loss in 2015, compared to a $367 million gain in 2014. The bottom line was hit by impairment of goodwill and acquired intangibles related to the company's exit from a majority stake in the marketing applications business.

Teradata generated $401 million in cash flow from operations in 2015, down from $680 million in 2014.

In 2016 Teredata reduced its cost structure and placed focus on its core data and analytics business.

As part of its focusing efforts, the agreed to sell its marketing applications business for $90 million to Marlin Equity Partners , an investment firm in April 2016. The deal is expected to be completed in 2016. The transaction includes Teradata's marketing resource management product

As part of the company's strategy around data warehousing and big data analytics, it invests in its core technology to extend its reach into additional market segments and to add product offerings, both through acquisitions and internal development. It also expands the services available on its platform through partnerships. In 2014 it expanded its partnership with MapR Technologies to further integrate MapR Distribution within Teradata's Unified Data Architecture.

In 2015 Teradata began offering Teradta Aster AppCenter, which offer applications that work with big data collected from diverse sources. The apps are designed for consumer finance, entertainment and gaming, healthcare, retail, and telecommunications.

Teradata made several acquisitions in 2014 to strengthen its big data offerings. In Revelytix, Teradata got information management products for big data with metadata management technology and expertise in integrating information across a company. In its acquisition of RainStor, Teradata bolstered its enterprise-grade Hadoop offerings, adding capabilities to add archival data storage capabilities, including data stored in Online transaction processing (OLTP), data warehouses, and applications. And the addition of Appoxee to the Teradata Integrated Marketing Cloud provides a full range of mobile capabilities.

Teradata became part of retail and financial systems maker NCR Corporation when AT&T bought both companies in 1991. AT&T spun off NCR, including Teradata, in 1996. More than a decade later, in 2007, NCR spun off Teradata as a separate, publicly traded company.
