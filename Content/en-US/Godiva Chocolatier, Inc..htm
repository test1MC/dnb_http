Riding into the hearts of chocolate aficionados worldwide, Godiva Chocolatier makes and sells premium chocolate concoctions, including bonbons, truffles, flavored coffee, cocoa mixes, cookies, ice cream, and liqueurs. Godiva's chocolate products are available in 100-plus countries through its more than 600 boutique stores, in department and specialty stores, through mail order, and from its website. The company began operations in North America in 1966 as an importer and distributor of Godiva chocolates from Belgium. It entered China in 2009 with a chocolate shop in Shanghai. Godiva is owned by Turkish confectioner Ülker Bisküvi Sanayi , which acquired it from Campbell Soup in 2008.

From its headquarters in New York, Godiva caters to chocoholics worldwide in more than 80 countries through its boutique shops, mail-order operation, website, and partner department and specialty stores. The company's flagship store on 5th Avenue in New York is its #1 store in North America.

The company operates in China, UK, Europe, Hong Kong, Japan, and Turkey.

In addition to its retail stores, the company has more than 2,000 wholesale locations. It offers more than 60 products, ranging from premium chocolates to biscuits, coffee, cocoa, cakes, chocolate bars, and chocolate drinks.

Godiva operates a pair of manufacturing facilities: one in Reading, Pennsylvania, and another in Brussels, Belgium. It also taps outside manufacturers globally to produce its products.

Its fiscal 2014 revenues totaled $759 million, driven by its efforts to enter new markets, including China, Indonesia, Korea, Macau, Saudi Arabia, Turkey, and Australia. International sales represented 52% of its total revenues.

Yildiz Holding of Turkey shelled out $850 million for the high-end chocolate maker. Godiva became part of Yildiz-owned Ülker , the largest food company in Turkey, with foodstuffs that include cookies, dairy products, and chocolate and other confectionery. Ülker, which ranks 31st on Istanbul's main ISE National 100 share index, asserts it controls about 50% of Turkey's confectionery market. While well-known in the Middle East, Ülker has ascended to the world stage as a player alongside the likes of Nestlé , Mars , Hershey , and Barry Callebaut . Previous parent Campbell purged Godiva to concentrate on its core products.

As part of a new organization but staying true to its roots, Godiva is looking to China's growing taste for luxury and labels as a lucrative market for its chocolate shops. It's achieving this by expanding beyond its presence in Shanghai to Beijing and Guangzhou. It's also expanding in the Middle East, Russia, and Turkey by opening some 50 new stores each year to reach sales of $1 billion by the end 2016.

In addition to tapping new markets, the company is transitioning its business from being focused on formal gifting to moving toward having more accessible products for self-consumption. Its vision statement is "To own the ultimate chocolate experience that inspires passion for our brand around the world."

Godiva continues to roll out new products and is concentrating on expanding its line of truffles. The chocolatier now makes individual chocolate bars, chocolate-covered fruits and nuts, and packaged chocolate biscuits. It also extended the reach of its liqueur line, made in conjunction with Diageo , by adding a caramel and milk chocolate product.

Specifically for the grocery market, Godiva sells a namesake coffee that comes in several varieties. They're sold through national retail food chains, including Safeway , Stop & Shop , and Giant . Available only through the Bakery Shop section on Godiva's website, the company sells chocolate brownies, cupcakes, and layered cakes. In 2014 it began to supplement its grocery store strategy by opening up chocolate shops inside Kroger stores; the first opened in Ann Arbor, Michigan, in 2014.

Godiva Chocolatier was founded more than 80 years ago in Brussels by master chocolatier Joseph Draps. The company was named in honor of the legend of Lady Godiva. Godiva was purchased by Campbell Soup Company in 1966 and planted roots in the US soon after.

It entered Paris in 1958, Tokyo in 1972, Hong Kong in 1997, China in 2009, and Turkey in 2010.
