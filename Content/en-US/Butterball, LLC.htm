At the Thanksgiving table, many consumers also give thanks to Butterball. The vertically integrated turkey processor (from egg to table) produces more than 1 billion pounds of turkey, including many of those that grace America's tables, for the retail grocery and foodservice industries in the US and 30-plus other countries. Butterball boasts the world's largest turkey-processing plant located in Mount Olive, North Carolina. The poultry processor also operates breeder, growing, and research farms; diagnostic labs; feed mills; and hatcheries. Through its purchase of Gusto Packing Co. , Butterball processes smoked bacon and pork products. Seaboard Corporation and Maxwell Farms each own 50% of Butterball.

The world's largest pork and hog producer, Smithfield Foods sold its 49% holding in Butterball in 2010 to majority owner Maxwell Farms (then at 51%) for $175 million. Subsequently, Maxwell Farms sold a 50% stake in the US's largest turkey producer to agribusiness giant Seaboard for $177 million.

From its headquarters in Garner, North Carolina, global company Butterball serves not only the US but also more than 30 other countries. It has operations in about half a dozen US states.

Butterball, which is the nation's largest turkey products producer, operates the largest turkey-processing plant in the world, measuring some 675,000 sq. ft. The North Carolina plant leads Butterball's production among its other production facilities in Missouri and Arkansas. Its Gusto Packing Co., purchased in 2012, has pork and turkey processing facilities in Montgomery, Illinois.

Butterball's business accounts for 20% of the total turkey production in the US.

The turkey processor serves several sectors, such as foodservice operators and retail stores.

Butterball has been putting down roots and streamlining its plant locations in recent years. The iconic turkey producer moved its corporate offices outside Raleigh, North Carolina, to be closer to its top production facility. Butterball also sold its Longmont operations, which produced turkey hot dogs, to Mexico's Sigma Alimentos . Butterball also decided to shutter its Colorado plant at the end of 2011, citing high feed costs.

To meet a rise in consumer demand for turkey burgers, Butterball in 2012 expanded its burger line and new tray pack and roll stock roast lines at its Carthage, Missouri, facility. The move helped to streamline operations.

The company works hard to maintain a diverse products portfolio. Some newer products include Butterball Ground Turkey in a chub package and Premium Turkey Franks. Frequently rolling out new items -- such as its cooked turkey breasts, turkey sausages, ground turkey, and fresh tray pack, bone-in and boneless turkey -- is part of the firm's commitment to giving traction to its brand name.

Butterball in 2013 acquired Illinois-based Gusto Packing Co. to provide the company with an additional 325 million pounds of processed capacity, thanks to Gusto's 260,000-sq.-ft. production plant. Known for its six basic product lines, Gusto gives Butterball added traction in expanding geographically for greater account penetration. Buying Gusto also gave Butterball a leg up in pork processing.

Butterball parent company Seaboard reported that its Turkey segment, which accounts for its investment in Butterball, logged $1.73 billion in revenue in fiscal 2013 -- a 20% increase from 2012's $1.44 billion.
