Formica Corporation knows a thing or two about the counter-culture. The Fletcher Building business unit designs, manufactures, and distributes its eponymous high-pressure laminate used for countertops, tables, floors, and other surfaces. Other products include fire-rated wall panels, sinks, doors, and toilet partitions. It also makes metal, stone, and wood veneer surface materials. In addition to residential kitchens, Formica's products are used in commercial buildings, including restaurants stores, offices, and hospitals. The company sells its products worldwide.

The company sell its products in Asia, Europe, and North America.

Formica is a part of Fletcher Building 's Laminates and Panels Division.

Formica sells its products through distributors, post formers, architects, and design specialists, as well as sales representatives.

The company's strategies for growth include expanding its offerings in Australia and New Zealand, where construction activities are expected to increase. Formica has discontinued less-popular products while offering innovations such as its 180fx granite slab-look countertops. It is also looking to become a greener business through environmentally conscious management of raw materials, waste, and energy.

In 2013 Formica introduced Formica Envision, available in both standard grade laminate and compact structural laminate. This new line provides the architectural and design community with a durable and affordable 'blank canvas' on which to replicate artwork, photography and graphics.

Formica has a partnership with leading suppliers, including North American edgebanding (used to cover the exposed sides of materials to give the appearance of a solid piece) providers, including Rehau and Dollken-Woodtape.

Expanding its asset base, in 2012 Formica acquired the remaining 50% it did not already own of Homapal Plattenwerk, a German-based maker and distributor of metal and specialty laminates.

Formica emerged from Chapter 11 bankruptcy protection in 2004 after spending two years reorganizing and restructuring. Private equity firms Cerberus Capital Management and Oaktree Capital Management sold Formica to Fletcher in 2007 for $700 million. Fletcher reported losses the following year -- largely due to Formica's performance -- a year that took its toll on construction and materials firms around the world. Formica is rebounding as demand improves, especially for its residential product lines.

Formica was founded in 1913 by engineers Daniel O'Conor and Herbert Faber. O'Conor's original patent was for a laminated material designed to serve the emerging electrical insulation industry. The mineral mica was commonly used at the time for electrical insulation; since the new invention worked as a substitute "for mica", the name Formica was born.
