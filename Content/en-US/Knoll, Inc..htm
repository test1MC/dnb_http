From the Bauhaus style to business chic, Knoll has designs on the furniture market. The company makes a variety of distinctively designed, curvilinear office furniture and related accessories, including office systems (aka cubicles). Its products are sold under such names as Equity, Antenna, AutoStrada, Reff Profiles, Morrison, Template, and Currents. Other items include ergonomic seating, tables and desks, and filing systems. Founded in 1938, it offers an upscale line of designed furniture (KnollStudio), computer and desk accessories (KnollExtra), and fabric and leather upholstery (KnollTextiles). The company markets its products directly and through some 300 independent dealers in North America and Europe.

The US accounts for about 90% of Knoll's sales. Europe is the furniture maker's second largest market, representing 8% of the companys's sales in 2015. Canada brings in the rest. Knoll has manufacturing facilities in Pennsylvania (1), Michigan (2), Italy (2), Illinois (1), Texas (1), and Canada (1). The company also sells furniture in Asia and Latin America.

Knoll operates three business segments. The largest -- Office -- accounted for 62% of 2015 sales. The office segment includes systems, seating, storage, tables, desks, and other accessories. The company's Studio segment includes its European subsidiary, KnollStudio, and Richard Schultz Design, and accounted for 28% of sales. The KnollStudio product portfolio includes lounge seating, cafe and dining chairs, bar stools, and conference, dining, and occasional tables. The KnollStudio line includes designs by such luminaries of architecture and design as Alvar Aalto and Ludwig Mies van der Rohe. More than 40 Knoll products are included in the permanent Design Collection of New York's Museum of Modern Art. The company also showcases its designs at the Knoll Museum, located in Pennsylvania near its headquarters. The firm's Coverings division(10%) includes KnollTextiles, Spinneybeck, Edelman Leather, which provide felt and leather textiles.

Knoll counts a variety of customers among its target audience. Primary clients include Fortune 1000 companies and government agencies, as well as medium- to large-sized organizations extending across several industries. The furniture retailer boasts a direct sales force of some 260 employees target this middle to upper end of the market.

The company sells its products primarily through a direct sales force, showrooms, and a broad network of independent dealers. In 2015, US federal, state, and local government agencies sales accounted for 10% of total sales.

Knoll is steadily recovering from the Great Recession in the US and global financial crisis, which greatly reduced the demand for office furniture and other Knoll products. The company's net revenue has been increasing in last three years (2013-15). In 2015 revenues grew by 5% due to an increase in Office segment and Studio segment sales.

Office segment revenues increased as a result of growth experienced across all of product categories, primarily through complementary products where the company has been aggressively investing.

Studio segment revenues grew, driven by strong sales volume growth in Holly Hunt Enterprises , one additional month of Holly Hunt sales included in 2015 and additional sales growth in the North American Studio business.

Knoll's net income increased by 42% due to higher net revenues and the absence of pension settlement and other post-employment benefit plans curtailment which it recorded in 2014.

In 2015 Knoll's operating cash inflow increased by 1%.

Knoll has built its business making and marketing office systems and specialty furniture, which generate about two-thirds of sales. In recent years, it has extended its reach into producing seating, files, storage, casegoods, and other specialty businesses. It is also looking to grw its international business. The move to diversify is intended to reduce Knoll's dependence on peddling office systems in North America and particularly the US.

In 2014 the company acquired Holly Hunt for $95 million, plus certain contingent payouts of up to $16 million. The acquisition helps the Knoll's strategy of building its global capability as a resource for high-design workplaces and homes, including the commercial contract, decorator to-the-trade and consumer markets.

German immigrant Hans Knoll founded Knoll in 1938 (starting in a single room on Manhattan's East Side that he dubbed Factory No. 1) on the premise that modern buildings would require modern furniture. Knoll and his wife, Florence, an architect, sought out noted architects to design the company's products: Eero Saarinen worked for Knoll, and the company later gained the rights to use designs by Ludwig Mies van der Rohe and Marcel Breuer. Knoll moved into textiles in 1947, the same time Hans decided to move manufacturing from New York City. He chose eastern Pennsylvania because of its large population of German immigrants.

Hans died in a car crash in 1955; his widow worked at Knoll for another decade. The company enjoyed the boom years of the 1950s and 1960s, but it ran into trouble when commercial construction dried up in the 1970s. General Felt acquired Knoll in the early 1980s, took it public in 1983, took it private again in 1986, and sold it to Westinghouse in 1990.

Knoll struggled during the early 1990s but eventually got back on track, redesigning its products, consolidating its manufacturing, and restructuring its management contracts. By the time Westinghouse sold Knoll to its top managers and merchant banker Warburg Pincus in 1996, the economy had improved and the company was profitable again.

The firm went public in 1997 with John Lynch (who previously worked at Westinghouse) as CEO. Continuing its tradition of hiring high-profile architects, the next year Knoll unveiled a line of furniture created by Vietnam War Memorial architect Maya Lin.

After settling a shareholder lawsuit to block the deal, Knoll returned to private ownership again in 1999 when Warburg Pincus and a Knoll management group bought the 40% of the company they didn't already own for nearly $500 million. COO Andrew Cogan replaced Lynch as CEO in 2001.

The company had a secondary public offering in 2006. Warburg no longer held an equity interest following the public offering.

With the economy rebounding from a major recession, and with it a growth in demand for office furniture, Knoll kicked off 2012 with a pair of acquisitions. The company acquired FilzFelt, a Boston-based supplier of German-milled 100% wool designed felt and floor coverings and architectural products. The purchase boosted Knoll's high-margin specialty business. It also bought Richard Schultz Design, which designs and makes outdoor furniture for residential, hospitality, and office furniture markets.
