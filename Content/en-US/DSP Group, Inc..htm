DSP Group loves the sound of its own voice ... chips. The company's name derives from the digital signal processors (DSPs) and related speech compression software it develops that convert speech and other audio data into digital values for cordless telephones, wireless phones, answering devices, PCs, and other consumer electronics. Top customers include landline makers VTech , Panasonic , Uniden , and CCT Telecom . More than 80% of the company's sales are to customers in Asia.

DSP operates through three divisions - home, office, and mobile. The home division, which accounts for 84% of sales, includes wireless ICs for cordless phones, residential gateway devices, wireless chipsets for home automation applications, and fixed-mobile convergence products. For the office segment, which accounts for 16% of sales, DSP makes VoIP terminals with converged voice and data applications. Its mobile products, which provide voice enhancement and far-end noise elimination for mobile phones and mobile headsets, have yet to make an impact on sales.

DSP Group does not manufacture its chips but instead contracts with Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Corp. (TSMC) to make its products. Testing and assembly is done by at Advanced Semiconductor Engineering , Giga Solutions, King Yuan Electronics , Siliconware Precision Industries , and Suzhou ASEN Semiconductors.

Hong Kong is its largest market, accounting for half of sales. Japan represents another 18%. Other Asian countries, such as China and Taiwan, make up another 10%. The US accounts for 2% of sales.

DSP Group has offices in China, Germany, Hong Kong, India, Israel, Japan, Scotland, South Korea, Taiwan, and the US.

The company uses both distributors and a direct sales force to sell its products. It has a limited number of customers; landline maker VTech accounts for 31% of sales, while Shenzhen Guo Wei Electronics and Panasonic represented 12% and 13%, respectively.

Overall sales fell 1% in 2015 to $144 million due to a 5% drop in its Home segment. A 56% growth rate in the miuch smaller Office segment kept the revenue reduction to just 1%. Demand for home phone systems declined, but office demand perked up from VoIP products.

Net income fell by more than half to $1.56 million in 2015 from $3.6 million in 2014. The company had higher R&D and sales and marketing expenses in 2015.

Lower inventory helped cash flow from operations rise to $12.2 million in 2015 from $10.4 million in 2014.

For future growth the company is especially hopeful about new products, such as Wi-Fi application devices, that serve home communication needs. Because a large percentage of the company's revenue comes from chipsets for fixed-line telephony, the company is also challenged with the task of developing new products as that technology is rapidly replaced by mobile telephony. DSP's XpandR-II chipset has been designed for use with Google 's Android.

Looking to expand beyond cordless phones, DSP is developing technology for the Internet of Things, smart home devices, and home gateways as well as VOIPO system-on-a-chip offerings for enteprise, small business, and home office applications.

The fabless semiconductor company combined its Ceva intellectual property licensing operation with Parthus Technologies in 2002 to form a separate company called ParthusCeva (now just plain CEVA ).

In 2003 DSP Group expanded its offerings by acquiring the assets of privately held Teleman Multimedia, which designed video compression and decompression chips. It also acquired the assets -- primarily intellectual property -- of privately held wireless chip designer Bermai in 2004.

In 2007 DSP Group acquired the Cordless & VoIP Terminals business of NXP for $270 million in cash and stock. NXP acquired a 14% equity stake in DSP Group as a result, a stake that DSP Group bought back in 2009 for about $20 million. The NXP business posted 2006 revenues of about $220 million. It sells radio-frequency (RF) chipsets, baseband semiconductors, software, and systems for a variety of communication applications.
