BlackBerry provides wireless hardware, software, and services worldwide. Its smartphones handle mobile voice, e-mail, and text messaging, as well as Internet access and multimedia applications. The company also provides software for managing mobile devices across a company and development tools. In addition to hardware sales, BlackBerry generates revenue from network access fees and software licensing fees. It sells to wireless carriers, resellers, and corporations. BlackBerry devices are offered by wireless network operators including AT&T Mobility , T-Mobile USA , Sprint , and Verizon Wireless . The company changed its name from Research in Motion in 2013.

BlackBerry products are sold in about 170 countries, but with 23% of the company's 2015 sales the US is its single biggest market. The UK and Canada round out the company's largest markets.

The mounting competition has taken its toll on BlackBerry's revenue and net income. The company's sales were $3.3 billion in 2015 (ended February) from $6.8 billion in 2014. Just three years ago the company had sales of $18.4 billion. In 2015, Blackberry faced intense competition, particularly from the introduction of the Apple iPhone 6 models during the year. Pricing adjustments made to maintain a user base resulted in fewer customers in higher revenue unlimited plans and more customers in prepaid and lower revenue plans. While the company posted a loss for 2015, it was $304 million, compared to the $5.8 billion loss of 2014. Blackberry slashed operating costs in 2015 by about $5 billion including big cuts in research and development and sales, marketing, and administration. Cash flow from operations was $813 million in 2015, a turnaround from a $159 million outflow in 2014.

Blackberry is aiming toward the enterprise mobility, leverage its technology for productivity and security to keep companies securely connected. It has organized into four business segments: Devices, Enterprise Services, Business Technology Solutions (BTS), and Messaging. The company is targeting businesses in regulated industries and vertical markets such as financial services, government, and healthcare. In addition to its telecom services, it seeks to provide application management, application enablement, and application development. Blackberry made deals with wireless companies T-Mobile US and Sprint in 2015. With T-Mobile, Blackberry brought its Blackberry Classic phone to T-Mobile's customers. With Sprint, Blackberry offers is Business Enterprise Services 12, an electronic mobility management system for enterprise communications.

Despite its financial woes, the company has made a small acquisitions to strengthen its technology. In 2015, Blackberry bought WatchDox, a data security company with file-sync-and-share technology. BlackBerry integrates WatchDox products as a service through BES12.

In 2014 it bought Secusmart GmbH, a German company that provides high-security voice and data encryption and anti-eavesdropping technology. The acquisition helps to position itself favorably with government customers and other corporate clients that use BlackBerry for secure communications. In 2012 it bought Paratek, whose antenna provides for faster data transfers and fewer dropped calls.
