If you design it, Nortech will come. The company offers outsourced electronics and cable assembly manufacturing to a variety of industries, such as aerospace, automotive, medical, and military contracting. Products include wire harnesses, printed circuit boards, electronic subassemblies, components, and more. A bevy of services includes design, testing, prototyping, and supply chain management. Most revenue comes from products following customer design specificacations. One customer accounts for more than a quarter of revenue.

Nortech receives 52% of revenue from industrial and commercial customers with medical and life sciences customers accounting for 36%, and aerospace and defense customers, 12%. General Electric has been a major customer of Nortech for years.

Nortech gets nearly all of its sales in the US. It has manufacturing operations in the US and in Mexico and China.

More business from industrial and commercial customers helped boost Nortech's revenue 3% to $115 million in 2015, a 3% increase from 2014. Aerospace and defense work dropped 3% while revenue from medical and life sciences was flat year-to-year.

The last time Nortech made more than $1 million in profit was in 2011. In 2015 the company slipped to a loss of $570,000 from a $840,000 profit in 2014. The company had higher costs in 2015, including expenses related to an acquisition.

Nortech sported strong cash flow at $3.4 million in 2015, compared to $1.6 million in 2014.

Nortech has invested in 2014 and 2015 to expand its manufacturing capabilities that will boost its international sales. It added significant space to its facility in Monterrey, Mexico, where the company has operated for 14 years. It began work in 2016 at a 27,000-sq.ft. facility in Suzhou China. The facilities allow Nortech to provide service from product concept and engineering to manufacturing and aftermarket.

The company said it would close its manufacturing facility in Augusta, Wisconsin by the end of 2016 because of slowing business from defense contractors.

Nortech acquired another Minnesota company, Devicix, in 2015 to extend its capabilities in regulated medical devices. The price was $5.1 million.
