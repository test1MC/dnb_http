Being in the line of fire is right where the folks at Task Force Tips want to be. The company manufacturers hand held and remote controlled fire suppression products such as nozzles, valves, hard suction hoses, and injection equipment that can deliver both water and foam. It also makes a wide range of repair kits for its various systems. It also offers tornado, hurricane, and monsoon remote control monitors. Other products include nozzles capable of spraying de-icing fluids (glycol and water) to aircraft surfaces or putting out oil refinery fires. About 10% of the Task Force Tips' employees are active firefighters and some 20% have been firefighters at some time in the past.

Task Force Tips sells its products to customers in 100 countries. The company's global salesforce is represented in 30 countries, with about 30% of the company's sales being made outside the US.

The company's distribution partners support its customers though product demonstration of Task Force Tips' equipment, technical and educational support, and service coordination.

Task Force Tips' serves a broad range of markets and segments, including industries (Petro-Chemical Processing, Storage and Transport, Nuclear Coal, Mining Transportation, Hydro Power Generation Transportation; Municipalities (Urban Departments, Suburban Departments, Rural Fire Departments, and Wildland Fire Agencies and Contractors); the Federal Government ( Department of Defense , Homeland Security , FEMA , and GSA Schedule 84 Cooperative Purchasing); Airmotive (Deicing/Anti-lcing companies); and International markets.

Task Force Tips claimed to generate $33 million in net sales for 2012.

The company manufactures fire-suppression nozzles suitable for use at oil refineries, a sector which has seen massive growth in the last few years. For this reason, Russia and the Middle East count among TaskForce Tips' biggest customers.

Task Force Tips was founded by Clyde McMillan as Fire Task Force Innovations in the basement of the McMillan family's home in Hobart, Indiana, in 1971. McMillan sketched his design for the first automatic nozzle on the back of a napkin in 1968; that napkin is now framed and on exhibit at the firehouse museum.
