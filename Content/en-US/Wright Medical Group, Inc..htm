Wright Medical Group Inc. makes replacement parts for humans. The firm makes reconstructive implants for the foot, ankle, hand, elbow, shoulder, and other defective joints. Product lines include the INBONE, CLAW, and ORTHOLOC systems for feet and ankles, MICRONAIL implants to repair wrist fractures. Wright makes an injectable putty for bone defects, as well as bone graft and tissue substitute materials, such as OSTEOSET pellets used to regenerate bone. The company sold its hip and knee implant business, OrthoRecon, in 2014. Wright's products are sold in more than 60 countries, although the US is its largest market. In 2015 the company merged with Netherlands-based Tornier to create Wright Medical Group N.V.

Wright Medical Group Inc. acquired Tornier in a $3.3 billion deal, after which it became a subsidiary of the Netherlands company (which was renamed Wright Medical Group N.V.). Through the deal, Wright Medical Group Inc. took 52% ownership of the combined company's stock. Wright Medical Group Inc. now serves as the US headquarters for the organization.

Wright Medical's surgical products for the fast-growing Foot and Ankle repair market account for more than 60% of annual sales. The company's Biologics business (25% of sales) supplies products to replace damaged or diseased bones and to stimulate bone growth. Wright's Upper Extremity unit (10% of sales) supplies hardware, including implants and other devices, to replace or reconstruct injured or diseased joints and bones in the hand, wrist, elbow, and shoulder.

Sales of products in the US market account for almost three-quarters of Tennessee-based Wright Medical's annual sales. European countries contribute more than 10% of revenues. The company also operates in Australia, Canada, and select Asian and Latin American countries.

Wright Medical sells its products in more than 60 countries through distributors and a direct sales force that targets customers in the orthopedic surgery market.

The specialty orthopedic company reported sales of $242.3 million in 2013, a 50% decline versus 2012. The plunge in sales was due to the sale of its hip and knee implant business, partially offset by a 41% increase in sales in Europe year over year stemming from the acquisition of UK-based WG Healthcare and France's Biotech International. When adjusted for the sale of the hip/knee implant unit (OrthoRecon), sales rose 13% in 2013 versus 2012. Sales of foot and ankle product rose 23%, while both the upper extremity and biologics segments posted 1% declines.

Wright Medical exited the hip and knee implant business with the sale of OrthoRecon as part of its strategy to transform itself into a profitable, high growth, pure play medical technology company. In time, Wright hopes to replace the substantial revenue and cash flow forfeited by the sale of OrthoRecon by ramping up high-margin revenue streams from extremities and biologics products. The company its overseas presence and product lines at home via acquisitions.

In January 2014, Wright completed the sale of its hip and knee implant business, OrthoRecon, to MicroPort for approximately $287.1 million. (OrthoRecon generated global annual revenue of about $232 million in 2013.) In January 2013, the company acquired WG Healthcare Ltd., a UK orthopedic extremities company, for about $7.6 million plus contingent considerations to be paid at a later date. Also in Europe, Wright acquired France's Biotech International, another extremities company, for approximately $75 million in cash and stock, plus additional milestone payments of up to $5 million cash payable in 2014 and 2015.

In 2013 Wright completed the acquisition of publicly traded, Tennessee-based BioMimetic Therapeutics . The $380 million deal ($190 million in cash and stock, plus additional milestones of up to $190 million) is intended to combine BioMimetic's biologics platform with Wright Medical's established product portfolio and sales force to push growth opportunities in the extremities market, specifically foot and ankle fusions. BioMimetic's Augment bone graft is approved in international markets and is pending approval in the US.

To widen its line of extremity devices in 2011, the company purchased the CCI Evolution mobile-bearing ankle replacement system from Van Straten Medical in a transaction worth some $6.9 billion.
