Peregrine Semiconductor's chips are made to take flight. The fabless company designs radio-frequency integrated circuits (RFICs) used in the wireless infrastructure, broadband, mobile wireless device, aerospace, and test and measurement markets. Its UltraCMOS technology combines standard complementary metal oxide semiconductor (CMOS) with a synthetic sapphire substrate that provides enhanced power and performance over silicon substrates. Other products include attenuators, mixers, and synthesizers. Peregrine Semiconductor generates more than 70% of sales from customers in Japan. It was acquired by Murata Manufacturing in late 2014.

As a fabless semiconductor company, Peregrine's outsourced manufacturing process allows it to focus on product design, development and support, and sales and marketing. It uses Kyocera , Namiki Precision Jewel Co., and Rubicon Technology to supply its sapphire substrates, a key raw material, while LAPIS Semiconductor of Japan, MagnaChip Semiconductor of South Korea, and Silanna Semiconductor of Australia make its products. Peregrine does some of its own final testing. Peregrine holds more than 180 filed and pending patents and has shipped more than 2.5 billion UltraCMOS units.

Outside the US, the company has offices in China, Japan, South Korea, and the UK. Japan is its largest market, accounting for more than 70% of sales. Customers in the US make up about 15% of sales.

Peregrine counts more than 1,500 customers but depends on a limited number each year for the majority of sales. Japanese semiconductor maker Murata Manufacturing accounted for almost 70% of sales in 2012. It mostly uses distributors - Japan-based Macnica and US-based Richardson Electronics are its two largest resellers.

The company is working to integrate additional RF components and circuits into a more complete system-on-chip (or single-chip) product, while looking to expand the adoption and use of its UltraCMOS technology through a licensing program.
