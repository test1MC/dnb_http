KMG Chemicals protects wood, helps make chips, and keeps the engines of industry humming. Its electronic chemicals are used in the manufacture of semiconductors. KMG's largest customer is silicon chip kingpin Intel , which regularly accounts for about 10% of total sales. Its wood preservatives are pentachlorophenol (penta), and sodium penta. KMG sells penta in the US, primarily to the railroad, construction, and utility industries. Sodium penta is sold in Latin America. In the industrial lubricants area, KMG makes and distributes industrial sealants, lubricants, and related equipment.

KMG produces and distributes specialty chemicals worldwide. Its two segments are Electronic Chemicals and Other Chemicals.

Electronic Chemicals (90% of the total revenue) sells high purity and ultra-purity wet process chemicals primarily to companies in the semiconductor market.

Other Chemicals (10%) includes its industrial lubricants and wood-treating chemicals businesses. In the industrial lubricants area, KMG makes and distributes industrial sealants, lubricants, and related equipment. Its wood treating chemicals line supplies penta (a preservative) to industrial customers to pressure-treat wood products (utility poles and crossarms).

The company's three operating subsidiaries are KMG Electronic Chemicals, KMG Val-Tex, and KMG-Bernuth.

Based in Fort Worth, Texas, KMG has other offices and facilities in Hollister, California; Houston, Texas; Tuscaloosa, Alabama; Pueblo, Colorado; Johor Bahru, Malaysia; Matamoros, Mexico; Milan, Italy; Riddings, the UK; Singapore; and Rousset, St. Cheron, St. Fromond, (France).

The US accounts 55% of KMG's total revenue.

The company sells its penta wood preservative products in the US, Canada, and Mexico.

The company sells products to 2,500 customers, primarily in the industrial lubricants market.

KMG sells to electronic chemicals customers via an internal sales force and distributors.

Other chemicals are sold through an internal sales force, distributors, and independent agents, and sold outside of the US via distributors and agents.

Intel accounts for about 10% of KMG's revenues.

KMG's revenues decreased by 7% in fiscal year 2016 (July year end) to $298 million.

Electronic chemicals segment sales dropped by or 1.5% primarily due to the impact the stronger US dollar on currency transactions (which cut sales by $6 million), and lower service sales in Asia (caused by the cessation of certain chemical service relationships). These negative impacts were largely offset by volume growth in North America.

Other chemicals revenue dropped by 33.4% in fiscal 2016 due to the divestiture of KMG's creosote business in early 2016.

The company reported a net income increase of 54% in fiscal 2016 to $18.7 million thanks to cost improvements from manufacturing consolidation in the electronic chemicals segment and the full year impact of sales in the industrial lubricants business (which was bought in May 2015). Gross profit as a percentage of sales increased in fiscal 2016 by 4% due to improved margins in electronic chemicals, operating efficiencies in KMG's electronic chemicals operations as a result of restructuring, and the realignment of its European operations.

The sale of the lower-profit margin creosote distribution business (in January 2015) also improved gross profit as a percentage of sales.

Cash from operations activities grew from $16.6 million in fiscal 2015 to $41 million in fiscal 2016 due to higher margins in the electronic chemicals and other chemicals segments. In addition, KMG paid $11.5 million less for distribution expense and $2.4 million less for taxes in fiscal 2016. Trade receivables declined by $5.2 million and inventories decreased by $4.3 million within the electronic chemicals segment. (Accounts payable and other accrued liabilities dropped by $5.3 million due higher-than-usual balances at the end of fiscal year 2015).

The company's strategy is to grow by discovering and exploiting opportunities that other companies overlook. It focuses on customer satisfaction, operational efficiencies, and optimizing its assets. KMG also seeks targeted acquisitions that mesh with and enhance existing business segments, and to effectively integrate acquisitions into ongoing operations while divesting non-core businesses.

Expanding it product line, in 2015 the company moved into the industrial lubricants market.

In 2015 KMG sold its creosote distribution business to Koppers for about $14.9 million.

Boosting its industrial lubricants portfolio, in 2017 KMG agreed to acquire Canada-based Sealweld, for $17.4 million.

Growing its electronic chemicals platform in Southeast Asia, in 2016, the company acquired Nagase Finechem Singapore for $2.9 million. The acquired company makes wet process chemicals, including solvents, acids and custom blends for the liquid crystal display, electronics and semiconductor markets, and provides recycling and refining services for certain customers.

In 2015, KMG bought Valves Incorporated of Texas, which makes and distributes industrial sealants and lubricants, primarily to the oil and gas storage, pipeline and gas distribution markets. It also sells related products, including lubrication equipment and fittings.

In 2013 KMG bought the Ultra Pure Chemicals subsidiaries of OM Group in the US, the UK, Singapore, and France for $60 million .

In 2012 the company sold its animal health business to Bayer Healthcare, including brands such as Patriot cattle ear tag and Rabon and Permectrin insecticides. Because KMG has grown significantly in the last five years in its electronic chemicals and wood-treating chemicals units, the company decided its animal health business no longer fit within its strategic objectives.

KMG acquired the electronic chemicals unit of General Chemical Performance Products in 2010 for $27 million. The unit manufactures wet process chemicals used primarily to clean and etch silicon wafers in the production of semiconductors. The deal not only increased KGM's electronic chemicals business but also expanding its reach into Asia markets.
