Tumi helps the well-heeled get where they're going in style. The high-end designer and manufacturer, named after an ancient ceremonial knife, makes travel gear (including suitcases and backpacks) and caters to corporate travelers with precious cargo, as the company has its own Tumi Tracer tracking system for retrieving lost bags. Tumi, which makes Ducati luggage under license, also offers laptop covers, wallets, belts, phone chargers, and other accessories. Its products are sold in the US (two-thirds of revenue)and in more than 75 other countries through about 177 of its own retail stores, websites, and upscale department and specialty stores. In 2016 luggage giant Samsonite International acquired the company for $1.8 billion.

Tumi's business is divided into four operating segments: Direct-to-Consumer North America, Direct-to-Consumer International, Indirect-to-Consumer North America, and Indirect-to-Consumer International.

The Direct-to-Consumer North America segment sells products directly to consumers through a network of company-owned retail stores consisting of full-price stores and outlet stores located in high-end retail malls or street venues. It also sells products to consumers through its e-commerce website. The segment generates almost half of revenue.

The Indirect-to-Consumer North America segment sells to wholesale customers, including specialty luggage retailers, prestige department stores, and business-to-business channels. Wholesale customers also sell Tumi products through their own e-commerce websites. Tumi also sells products in partner stores operated by local distributors or retailers that carry only Tumi products and are governed by strict operating guidelines dictated by Tumi. It accounts for a fifth of revenue.

The Direct-to-Consumer International segment sells directly to consumers through a network of company-owned full-price and outlet stores in high-end street venues and select malls in international locations. Tumi sells products directly to consumers through its e-commerce website. It brings in about 6% of revenue.

The Indirect-to-Consumer International segment sells products through wholesale distribution channels across Europe, the Middle East, and Africa; the Asia-Pacific region; and Central and South America. As part of this business, products are sold through partner stores operated by local distributors or retailers that carry only Tumi products and are governed by strict operating guidelines dictated by Tumi. It also operates concessions in department stores throughout Europe and the Middle East. (Many Tumi wholesale customers also operate their own e-commerce websites to peddle Tumi-branded products.) The segment is responsible for about a quarter of revenue.

Based in New Jersey, Tumi operates its business worldwide. North America accounts for 66% of its 2015 sales, followed by China (4%) and the rest of the world, 30%.

Tumi distributes its products in more than 75 countries through some 2,000 points of distribution. The multichannel marketer sells through company-owned retail and outlet stores, partner stores, third-party wholesale, e-commerce, and special markets channels.

Tumi reported increase financials across the board for 2015.

The company posted a 4% increase in revenue to $547 million in 2015 from $527 million in 2014. An 8% increase in Direct-to-Consumer North America sales added $19 million to the company's top line. Sales also rose in its geographic markets.

Net income rose 9% to $63 million in 2015 from $58 million in 2014 on higher sales and proceeds from a joint venture with Tumi Japan.

Cash generated by operations reached $81 million in 2015 from $60.5 million in 2014.

Tumi has expanded its retail business with some 70 new stores opened in the past three years.

The company in 2016 bought the half of Tumi Japan that it didn't own and now has control of its operations in the country. The Japan operation has 13 stories and another 150 points of sale.

Aiming to take a larger share of the luxury goods market, Tumi has been positioning itself as a lifestyle brand in recent years. It makes a line of motorcycle-inspired bags through a partnership with Ducati , sells Tumi-branded activewear via a deal with Umbro , collaborates with Germany's Carl Zeiss for lenses that can repel dirt, dust, oil, and water, and pumps out travel speakers and phone chargers. The company also unveiled its Traverso collection of eyewear in 2011. By expanding its accessory offerings, Tumi believes it can better compete with well-known luxury brands, such as Coach and Louis Vuitton .

Charlie Clifford founded Tumi in 1975 after a Peace Corps stint in Peru.
