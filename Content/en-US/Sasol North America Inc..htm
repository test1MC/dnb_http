Being all over the map is just fine with a company like Sasol Chemicals (USA) (formerly Sasol North America), which is a part of South African diversified chemical giant Sasol Limited 's Olefins and Surfactants unit (which itself is based in Germany). The USA division produces a number of commodity and specialty chemicals from its manufacturing sites and an R&D center in the US. Among the products made by Sasol Chemicals (USA) are alcohols, alumina, paraffins, specialty surfactants, and fatty acid esters. Sasol Chemicals (USA) has built its operations on what was once ConocoPhillips ' chemicals unit.

Its plants are located in Maryland, Louisiana, Arizona, Pennsylvania, and Texas; it also has offices in Hayward and Richmond (both in California) and Calgary, Canada.

Sasol Chemicals (USA)'s manufacturing operations produce a range of applications, including cleaners and detergents, cosmetics, personal care, pharmaceutical, food, textile, leather processing, and heat transfer fluids.

The company sells its products via sales and distribution centers. Sasol Chemicals (USA)'s products are used in a range of industries, including Agrochemicals, Alumina, Detergents, Enhanced oil recovery, Industrial and Institutional, Metalworking and Lubricants, Oilfield Chemicals, and Personal Care.

In fiscal 2015, the company's net revenues decreased by 1% due to the decline in global commodity prices.

Sasol Chemicals (USA) traces its origins to ConocoPhillips ' chemicals unit, which was spun off as a private company (Vista Chemical) in 1984. It went public two years later, was purchased by RWE in 1991, and was sold to Sasol in 2001.

In 2013 Sasol Chemicals (USA) selected Univation Technologies, UNIPOL PE Process for its petrochemical project in Lake Charles. The new polyethylene plant will be capable of producing a broad product portfolio including metallocene polyethylene. Parent Sasol has a long-standing relationship with Univation, which has worked with the company in developing a similar plant in South Africa.

Responding to the growth in the North American shale gas liquids market (and subsequent expansion of available ethane), in 2011 Sasol began a feasibility study for a major ethylene cracker and derivatives complex at Sasol North America's complex in Southwest Louisiana. The cracker unit could produce between 1 million tons and 1.4 million tons of ethylene per year and would cost between $3.5 billion to $4.5 billion to build.
