InvenSense is ready to move technology beyond point and click. The semiconductor manufacturer offers motion, sound, and directional interface sensors used to detect, track, and assess a user's position and other physical characteristics. Its MotionTracking sensors, which include MEMS (microelectromechanical systems) gyroscopes and motion measurement processing units, are used in consumer devices such as smartphones, tablets, wearables, gaming consoles, and smart TVs to provide a more immersive user interface. InvenSense outsources manufacturing to contractors. It generates the majority of sales in Asia. In 2016 InvenSense agreed to be bought by TDK for $1.3 billion. The deal is expected to close in 2017.

The $1.3 billion acquisition of InvenSense by TDK adds InvenSense's wide range of sensor technology to TDK's lineup of magnetic sensors, which it developed over the years with its work with hard disk drives. The acquisition is to strengthen TDK's product line of sensors for the internet of things (IoT), automotive, and information and communications technology applications.

InvenSense generates about two-thirds of sales from makers of smartphones and tablets, with revenue from optical image stabilization, internet of things, and other applications supplying the rest. The company's devices are made by Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Corp. and GlobalFoundries .

Headquartered in California, InvenSense operates a wafer sort and testing plant in Taiwan, research and development offices in the Slovak Republic, Canada, France, Italy, and Massachusetts, and sales offices in China, Japan, and South Korea. Customers in the US account for about 45% of sales.

InvenSense uses a direct sales force as well as an indirect channel of distributors, who sell to manufacturers of consumer electronics devices, original design manufacturers, and contract manufacturers. Apple and Samsung are InvenSense's biggest customers, accounting for about half of the company's sales. Other customers include Fitbit , Roku , Google , Black & Decker , Amazon.com , and Tesla Motors .

InvenSense's revenue rose 12% to $418 million in 2016 (ended April). The company received boosts from increased adoption of its optical image stabilization technology in smartphone cameras and the increasing adoption of devices for internet of things (IoT) market. The IoT market was particularly strong, growing 115% year-to-year. The smartphone business suffered a 20% revenue drop because of a fall off in 2016 sales from a key customer that introduced a new smartphone in 2015.

The company posted a net loss of $21 million in 2016 up from a $1 million net loss in 2015. The company had higher costs in research and development from additional headcount from acquisitions and in sales and marketing. The company also paid about $12 million to settle patent litigation brought by Robert Bosch , a competitor.

InvenSense reported cash from operations of $83 million in 2016, compared to $67 million in 2015. Non-cash expenses of about $71 million consisted of stock-based compensation of $35 million, depreciation and amortization of $22 million, non-cash interest expense of $8 million, and deferred income tax assets of $10 million. The company also had changes in operating assets and liabilities and decreases in inventories and accounts receivables.

Putting on its marketing hat, InvenSense has given a different name to the internet of things. It's calling it the internet of sensors, which fitst with it product line, but is accuratet. Sensors are the devices that connect the things -- toasters, digital assistants, smart home devices, appliances, weather buoys, autos, and more -- to the internet. InvenSense's products fit that bill, capturing and translating real world data to digital bits. InvenSense's sales for the internet of things market surged 115% in 2016 and the company has laid plans to provide sensors for more devices. It is developing products for automotive applications in electric vehicles and self-driving vehicles, industrial robots, drones, and virtual and augmented reality devices.

The company has expanded its product portfolio to include software, fingerprint, and automotive technology offerings and has set up two entities to develop and sell them. UltraPrint is its ultrasonic fingerprint authentication product, which is uses InvenSense's proprietary MEMS fabrication platform for Piezoelectric Micromachined Ultrasonic Transducers. Coursa is a cloud-based motion data platform that enables analysis and understanding of user activities and retail shopper behavior.

A recent acquisition was Spirit, which brings navigation technology to InvenSense.
