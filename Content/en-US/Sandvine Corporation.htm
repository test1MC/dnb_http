You might have heard something through the Sandvine, a Canadian company that designs and makes equipment used by broadband service providers to better manage peer-to-peer network traffic. In addition to traffic management, the company's products enable network administrators to manage network security features. Its network policy control products are deployed in more than 250 networks in some 90 countries. Sandvine serves clients including Hughes Network Systems and has sales partnerships with companies such as Telindus .

Sandvine generates nearly two-thirds of its revenue from the sale of it hardware and software products with the rest coming from the services it provides to customers. The company researches, designs, and makes its products with some sub-assemblies made by by contract manufacturers. It generally carries a good amount of inventory because of the variability in product mix and a long lead time for parts. The company balances the cost of stocking up on parts with the ability to make quick deliveries to customers.

The biggest chunk of Sandvine's sales are made to customers in the Europe, Middle East and Africa region at 36%. Shipments to the US account for 28% of revenue, followed by the Caribbean and Latin America, 18%, and Asia-Pacific, 13%.

The company conducts its major operations at its Waterloo, Canada headquarters. It also works with a contract research and development facility in India.

Sandvine sells through its own sales staff and through distributors. Its indirect sales channels include global network equipment resellers as well as regional value-added resellers.

In 2015 (ended November) no customer accounted 10% or more of Sandvine's revenue. In 2014, Alcatel-Lucent accounted for 14% of Sandvine's revenue and Juniper Networks accounted for 10% of revenue.

Sandvine's revenue dipped a bit in 2015 (ended November), down 0.05% as an increase in service revenue didn't quite make up for a drop in product revenue. Sales dropped 23% in the Asia-Pacific region.

Net income, however, jumped to $41 million in 2015, an $18 million improvement from 2014. Sandvine has lower research and development costs and a lower provision for income taxes in 2015.

Mergers and Acquisitions
