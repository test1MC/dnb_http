Like to read but hate turning those pesky pages? Franklin Electronic Publishers may have a solution for you. The company markets handheld, electronic devices (which are made by third parties) that display the text of reference and educational publications in four areas: Language Expansion, Language Learning, Reading Enhancement, and Writing Enhancement. It owns or licenses more than 110,000 electronic titles, including Merriam-Webster's Collegiate Dictionary, Bartlett's Familiar Quotations , and the Holy Bible , which can be downloaded to Franklin's handheld devices and those of other manufacturers. Products are available in 16 languages at nearly 50,000 retail outlets worldwide. The company was founded in 1981.

Barry Borden, Russell Bower, and Joel Shusterman founded Franklin Computer as a PC maker in 1981. Despite initial success, the company soon ran into a legal buzz saw when it was hit with a $2.5 million judgment in a copyright infringement suit brought by Apple Computer (now called Apple). The company filed for bankruptcy in 1984, and Morton David replaced Borden as CEO. Emerging in 1985, Franklin began concentrating on handheld computers.

In 1986 the company went public and introduced its Spelling Ace dictionary. It adopted the Franklin Electronic Publishing name in 1990. Two years later Franklin launched its first medical product, the Med-Spell dictionary. It introduced its BOOKMAN electronic book product line in 1995. Franklin acquired a license to sell personal organizers under the ROLODEX trademark in 1996. It unveiled its REX personal organizer the following year.

David resigned as CEO in 1997. Andrew Cross replaced him in 1998 but was replaced by Barry Lipsky the following year. The company sold the REX product line to Xircom in 1999.

In 2000 Franklin debuted its eBookMan handheld electronic book reader. The company also began offering downloadable texts from its Web site and struck a deal with Hong Kong's Group Sense to design and market electronic books and other devices in China.

In 2002 the company agreed to distribute electronic books for the Time Warner Book Group .

In 2005 Franklin sold its 25% stake in France's MobiPocket (a company working on software that allows users to download a single file of electronic content to any platform) to Amazon.com .

The company was taken private in a management-led buyout in 2010.
