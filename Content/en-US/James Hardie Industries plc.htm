James Hardie Industries takes the rap for wrapping homes in fiber cement siding. A pioneer in cellulose-reinforced fiber cement, building materials company James Hardie uses the material to create products for residential and commercial construction, including siding (Hardiplank), external cladding, walls, fencing, and roofing. The company's largest segment, USA Fibre Cement, has made substantial inroads into the US siding market, where it is now one of the largest siding producers. Hardiplank siding has gained popularity for its durability and low maintenance. James Hardie operates in the US, Canada, Europe, the Philippines, Australia, and New Zealand.

James Hardie works in two segments based somewhat on geography. The North America and Europe Fiber Cement segment makes fiber cement interior linings, exterior siding products, and related accessories in the US. That segment generates 80% of the company's revenue. The other segment covers James Hardie's operations in the Asia/Pacific region and it accounts for the other 20% of revenue. Products are made in Australia and the Philippines and sold in those countries and in New Zealand, throughout Asia, and the Middle East.

Breaking down James Hardie's sales by region and country makes it clear where the Dublin-based company generates most of its revenue. The North America region supplies 78% of the company's revenue, followed by Australia, 13%, and New Zealand, 4%. All other countries combined bring in 5% of its revenue.

The company operates nine manufacturing plants in the US, two in Australia, and one each in New Zealand and the Philippines. James Hardie obtains silica quartz for its products from leased mines in Tacoma, Washington, Reno, Nevada, and Victorville, California.

James Hardie sells through a two-step process in the US. For the new construction market, the company sells to distributors who sell to dealers or lumberyards. The company sells through home-improvement stores and specialist distributors to reach the repair-and-remodel market.

In Australia and New Zealand James Hardie sells to distributors and hardware stores and lumber yards for the new construction and repair-and-remodel markets. In the Philippines, the companies goes through a network of thousands of small- to medium-sized dealer outlets for all markets.

James Hardie posted a 4% revenue gain for 2016 (ended March), hitting $1.73 billion, compared to $1.66 billion in 2015. In the US and Europe segment the company reported a 9% rise on higher volumes from the new construction and repair-and-remodel markets. A price increase set at the beginning of the 2016 fiscal year also played a part in boosting revenue. In the Asia/Pacific market revenue dropped 10% for 2016, which the company blamed on the exchange rate between the strong US dollar and the weaker Australian dollar. The segment also had higher operating costs, which were related to the start of a new production facility.

Net income fell 16% in 2016 from 2015, dropping to $244 million from $291 million because of the impact of higher expenses and currency exchange rates.

Cash inflow from operations was $260 million in 2016, compared to $179.5 million in 2015 because of changes in working capital from timing in accounts receivable and accounts payable.

James Hardie has been increasingly focusing on its diverse international markets, allowing it to be less dependent on the ebbs and flows of one or two economies. The company spent $89 million (Australian) to increase by 40% the capacity of its Carole Park manufacturing facility. The company continually develops new construction materials. It spends around $30 million a year on research and development with some $7.5 million going to commercialization projects.

The company traces its roots to 1888, when a young Scottish man named James Hardie emigrated to Australia and started a tannery business.
