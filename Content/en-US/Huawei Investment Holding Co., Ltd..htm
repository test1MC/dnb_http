A cell phone call could travel completely over Huawei Technologies products, except for the air between cell towers. The Chinese tech giant competes with Ericsson , Cisco Systems , and Nokia in making telecommunications and network infrastructure and with Samsung and Apple in making smartphones. It also makes equipment for corporate IT systems and cloud computing. The company has been a well-known presence n China, but it has aggressively expanded to other markets in recent years. Telecom carriers such as Telefonica , Vodafone , and Telstra are Huawei's biggest customer set, but its consumer unit is growing fast (it sold 108 million smartphones in 2015, third in the world). Founded in 1987, the company is owned by its 170,000 employees.

Huawei's carrier business group targets telecom carriers with a broad array of communications equipment that includes 4G and 4.5G LTE, GSM, cloud and data center hardware and software, switches, software, and secure gateways. The unit also offers global services that include consulting, systems integration, energy retrofits, and managed services. The carrier business brought in about 59% of the company's revenue in 2015.

For consumers Huawei makes a range of smartphones that run on Google's Android operating system. Lower-cost units dominate its handset mix with mid-range to high-priced phones accounting for just 30% of smartphone sales, although it is raising its sights to higher-value units. The consumer business, which includes smart watches and fitness trackers, generated a third of revenue in 2015.

The enterprise unit is home to Huawei's cloud computing, data center, and IT products and services. Besides making computing and storage devices, the company develops technologies around software defined networks and network functions virtualization for greater capacity and flexibility. The unit focuses on the public security, finance, transportation, and energy markets. Huawei got 7% of its 2015 revenue from the enterprise business.

Huawei was founded and grew up in China from its headquarters and base in the Shenzhen technology manufacturing hub. China remains the company's strongest market, accounting for 42% of revenue in 2015. The market grew 54% from year-to-year. The Europe-Middle East-Africa (EMEA) market generates about a third of revenue on 27% growth in 2015. The Americas revenue rose 26% in 2015 and accounts for 10% of revenue. Asia-Pacific makes up 13% of sales.

Huawei works with a number of partners to develop and sell its products and services. The enterprise unit, for example, has more than 8,000 channel partners and 350 solution partners.

In its consumer business Huawei's revenue from open channels, which includes e-commerce, rose 130% in 2015 from 2014 and made up 58% of consumer revenue. The company operates more than 53,000 retail stores in 100 countries.

Huawei reported higher revenue and profit in 2015.

Revenue reached $60.8 billion in 2015, a 37% increase from 2014. The company grew from carrier's building out 4G networks around the world; infrastructure construction and installations in India, the Philippines, Thailand, and Central and South America; and rising smartphone sales in the US.

Huawei's profit rose to $5.68 billion in 2015 from $4.3 million in 2014. The company credited higher revenue and greater operating efficiency for the profit increase. While watching costs, Huawei increased investment in research and development and brand marketing in 2015. R&D spending, in particular, increased some 45% year-to-year.

Cash flow from operations increased to $7.6 billion in 2015 from $6.4 billion in 2014.

Huawei's strategy for growth begins with its technologies. It is developing new generations of products throughout its large stable of telecom equipment, from smartphones to mobile broad band to cloud computing. The company calls this its pipe strategy, which endeavors to pump products for Internet, Communications, and Telecommunications (ICT) through the pipeline from R&D to sales and marketing to customers.

While many of the company's products come from its 16 R&D centers around the world, Huawei works closely with partners and customers to develop products and services in what is calls an open ecosystem. In its cloud business, for example, Huawei works with some 500 partners.

In 2006, 3Com agreed to buy out Huawei's 49% stake in their joint venture, Huawei-3Com (renamed H3C Technologies ), for $882 million; the deal closed early the following year. Later in 2007, however, Huawei announced plans to purchase a stake in 3Com concurrent with that company's acquisition by Bain Capital . The deal stalled in 2008 when 3Com withdrew its application to the Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States (CFIUS). 3Com was unable to satisfy national security concerns raised by the US government over Huawei's investment.

In 2009 TeliaSonera installed 4G equipment from Huawei to support service in Oslo, Norway. Later that year, Net4Mobility (a joint venture of Tele2 and Telenor ) selected Huawei to provide 4G infrastructure equipment and modems for a nationwide 4G network in Sweden.

In 2011 Huawei bought wireless semiconductor start up M4S from Belgium-based mobile networking products maker Option to expand its internal wireless component development activities.
