Xilinx gives control to the programmer on the ground. The company is a top supplier of field-programmable gate arrays (FPGAs) and complex programmable logic devices (CPLDs). Customers program -- and reprogram -- these integrated circuits (ICs) to perform specific functions, providing greater design flexibility and cutting time to market. Xilinx also offers a broad range of design software and intellectual property used to customize its chips. The company, which contracts with third-party manufacturers to produce its chips, sells to the automotive, aerospace, broadcast, consumer, data processing, and wired and wireless communications markets. More than 70% of revenue comes from customers outside the US.

Xilinx groups its products by where they are on the continuum of development, from newest to oldest. Sales from New Products, which are the company's latest creations, contribute 44% of total sales. Sales of its oldest products, the Base Products segment, accounts for 28% of revenue while Mainstream Products, which aren't as old as Base products, are about a quarter of sales. About 4% comes from Support Products.

Xilinx, which is headquartered in the US, has facilities in Singapore, Canada, China, India, Ireland, Scotland, Sweden, and the US. The Asia/Pacific region, including Japan, accounted for almost 50% of its net sales in 2016 (ended April). Other major markets are the US (27% of revenue) and Europe (19% of revenue).

Xilinx has sales offices worldwide. In addition to direct sales, global mega-distributor Avnet accounted for 50% of the company's revenue. The communications and data center markets generated 41% of its net revenues for 2016; the industrial, aerospace, and defense sector contributed 42%; and broadcast, consumer, and automotive accounted for 17%.

Xilinx reported lower revenue and profit in 2016 (ended April) as sales of its older products continued to erode.

Sales of the company's new products grew 20% in 2016, which helped to hold revenue to a 7% drop to $2.2 billion, compared to about $2.4 billion in 2015. Sales fell in all geographic markets except in parts of North America outside the US. Lower sales in China and Europe wiped out about $100 million in revenue compared to 2015.

The company's profit fell 15% to $550.8 million in 2016 from $791 million in 2015.

The company generated about $730 million from operations in 2016, compared to $791 million in 2015. It was the second straight year for a drop in cash flow.

The semiconductor industry has gone through a wave of consolidation in the past two years. It included Altera , a chief Xilinx rival, being bought by chip giant Intel for some $16.7 billion. Intel is integrating Altera's FPGA capabilities into some of its products.

In 2016, the company introduced 16 nanometer Zynq UltraScale+ portfolio of devices. The devices provide 64-bit processor scalability and combine real-time control with soft and hard engines for graphics, video, waveform, and packet processing.

Xilinx scaled up R&D by 2% in 2016 to $534 million from 2015. Its lab efforts are directed to developing software design automation tools for hardware and embedded software. The company intends to spend even more on R&D in 2017 and focus on its 16nm portfolio roll out, early investment on 7nm development, and creating a 28nm Spartan 7 product portfolio.

In 1984 Bernie Vonderschmitt, Ross Freeman, and Jim Barnett started Xilinx when ZiLOG , their employer, rejected the idea of investing in a programmable chip operation. Xilinx introduced the first field-programmable gate array (FPGA) the next year. Successive products featured more logic gates, providing greater computing power. A chip with up to 5,000 usable logic gates came out in 1987; three years later that number quadrupled. (Freeman died in 1989; Barnett left the company in 1990, the year Xilinx went public.)

Xilinx acquired FPGA software developer NeoCAD in 1995. That year Xilinx formed a joint venture with United Microelectronics to build a wafer operation, United Silicon, in Taiwan. Xilinx brought in Hewlett-Packard veteran Willem (Wim) Roelandts as CEO in 1996, replacing Vonderschmitt, who remained chairman until his retirement in 2003. (Roelandts then succeeded him in the chairman's role as well; Vonderschmitt died the next year.)
