Biosense Webster makes products that explore, and in some cases alter, the terrain of the human heart. Its catheters and imaging systems are used by heart specialists worldwide to find and treat heart arrhythmias (irregular heart rhythms). Its CARTO electrophysiology (EP) navigation systems uses 3-D imaging technology to map the heart's electrical activity and lets doctors navigate the heart using the company's diagnostic catheters. Biosense Webster also makes therapeutic ablation catheters and systems that destroy faulty electric pathways in the heart that cause arrhythmias. Biosense Webster is a part of Johnson & Johnson 's Medical Devices segment.

The company has operations in 40 countries and its products are sold in nations around the globe. It operates customer support and training centers in Cincinnati, Ohio; Hamburg, Germany; and Shanghai, China.

Biosense Webster often uses strategic alliances with other health care firms to advance its product development and to expand its product offerings. Through a distribution deal with Siemens Medical Solutions , for example, the company sells Siemens' AcuNav ultrasound catheter to the electrophysiology and interventional cardiology markets. It also sells Stereotaxis ' Odyssey workflow management systems for electrophysiology labs.

Biosense Webster has other collaborations with companies (including Medtronic , GE Healthcare, Siemens Healthcare , and Stereotaxis) through which it has integrated its technologies with products made by partnering firms. For example, Biosense Webster's ThermoCool magnetic catheters (used to treat irregular heartbeats) were developed for use with Stereotaxis' Niobe magnetic navigation systems.

To support its customers, Biosense Webster provides product training for electrophysiologists and other medical professionals via the Internet and through three education centers in China, Germany, and the US. The company also provides procedural coding and insurance reimbursement assistance.

Launches of new ThermaCool catheter products in 2012 led to strong sales growth in the Biosense Webster electrophysiology business.

The company's products are distributed to wholesalers, hospitals, and retailers. They are used principally by physicians, nurses, therapists, hospitals, diagnostic laboratories, and clinics. Biosense Webster's marketing efforts specifically target electrophysiologists and interventional cardiologists.

Biosense Webster launches new products developed through internal research and development programs, as well as through R&D partnerships with other companies. The company also works to expand approved uses for existing products. Focus areas of development include diagnostic and treatment devices for atrial fibrillation, atrial flutter, ventricular tachycardia, and other cardiovascular conditions. It is working to enhance electrophysiology systems with real-time 3D technology that allows for fast, precise diagnosis and treatment of disease.

In addition to product development deals, Biosense Webster works to expand distribution deals with other manufacturers. Such partnerships enhance the company's capabilities to provide end-to-end solutions for cardiac customers.

Biosense Webster was formed in 1999 after J&J bought Webster's parent (cardiac device maker Cordis ) and Biosense, and then combined the two firms.
