The world's #1 manufacturer of car safety equipment, Autoliv aims to save lives by increasing the survivability statistics of traffic accidents. It makes components such as seat belts, airbags, anti-whiplash systems, and safety electronics. Other products include rollover protection systems, steering wheels (with airbags), night vision systems, radar systems, and child seats. The company caters to about every car maker in the industry and has more than 100 locations around the globe. Car making giant GM is one of its largest customers. Autoliv was established in 1956.

Autoliv operates through four primary segments: airbags and associated products (55% of total sales), seat belts and associated products (28%), passive safety electronic products (10%), and active safety products (7%).

Beginning in 2015, the company launched a new internal structure consisting of two business segments: passive safety (including airbags and seatbelts) and electronics (including passive safety electronics and active safety), to manage its business operations.

Autoliv has 20 crash test tracks, 20 technical centers, and 78 production facilities in 27 countries. Its US operations are overseen by Autoliv ASP, Inc. Its largest markets are the Americas (35% of total sales), Europe (31%), and Asia (34%).

General Motors is Autoliv's largest customer, accounting for 12% of sales in 2015, while Ford and Renault/Nissan accounted for 12% and 10% of sales, respectively.

After posting a revenue milestone of $9.24 billion in 2014, Autoliv saw its revenues dip slightly to $9.17 billion in 2015. The marginal decrease was mainly due to unfavorable foreign currency effects primarily in Japan and Europe. Excluding foreign currency effects, net revenue increased by 8% in 2015 due to a spike in organic growth in both the segments.

Organic sales for passive safety electronics products (mainly airbag control modules and remote sensing units) grew primarily from higher sales in China and South Korea in 2015. The company also experienced higher sales of replacement inflators, inflatable curtains, and steering wheels; in addition, seatbelt products sales climbed in Europe and North America. Autoliv in 2015 experienced a strong increase in sales of active safety products (automotive radars, night vision systems, cameras with driver assist systems, positioning systems and brake control systems) resulting from higher sales of radar related products.

Autoliv's profits remained consistent for both 2014 and 2015, hovering around the $460 million mark. Its operating cash flow has fluctuated over the last five years; after decreasing in 2014, it jumped by 5% in 2015.

The company is focused on growing by increasing its organic sales overseas. In 2014 the company began production on two of its largest investments, a propellant plant and a textile center, in China. A major advantage to expansion outside of its traditional market of North America is that cars built in developing economies have tended to lightly adopt safety features -- so there is room for further penetration.

In 2015, the company acquired the automotive solutions business (GPS and its technical, commercial and manufacturing support) of Massachusetts based- M/A-COM Technology Solutions Holdings , a supplier of high-performance analog RF, microwave, millimeter wave and photonic semiconductor products, for $100 million in cash. The acquisition expanded the company's growing position in the active safety market and provided additional building blocks to its portfolio in automated driving.

Autoliv traces its origins back to 1956 when Autoliv AB, a Swedish corporation, pioneered automotive seat belt technology. By 1967, the company had invented the retractor belt. Granges Weda AB, another maker of seat belt retractors, acquired the company in 1975. Electrolux bought the Granges Group (later renamed SAPA) in 1989 and changed its name to Electrolux Autoliv. Throughout the 1980s and 1990s, the company continued to grow through acquisitions, buying seat belt manufacturing operations primarily in Europe, but also in Australia and New Zealand. In 1994 the company changed its name to Autoliv AB and went public, with Electrolux selling all its shares during the offering.
