MOCON makes precision instruments that help you look before you leak. Its products include permeation and packaging instruments that measure the rate at which oxygen, carbon dioxide, and water vapor penetrate packaging. The company also makes materials analyzers that measure the thickness of coatings and thin films. Such products are used by packagers in the food and beverage, pharmaceuticals, and chemical industries, and by paper, plastics, and coatings manufacturers. MOCON also makes pharmaceutical capsule and tablet weighing and sorting devices, automatic sample preparation systems, and offers related consulting and development services. More than half of sales come from outside the US.

MOCON operates through three divisions: package testing products, permeation products, and industrial analyzers. Package testing products (45% of sales) consist of headspace analyzers, leak detection equipment, and gas mixers sold under the brands PAC CHECK, CheckMate, CheckPoint, LeakMatic, MAP Mix, and MAP Check 3.

Permeation products (40% of sales) measure the rate at which gases and vapors transmit through materials. Its product portfolio consists of the AQUATRAN, OX-TRAN, PERMATRAN-W, and PERMATRAN-C series.

Finally, industrial analyzers (15% of sales) consist of gas chromatograph and total hydrocarbon analyzers sold under the brands BEVALERT, PETROALERT, and piD-TECH,as well as its GreenLight line of microbial detection products.

MOCON's products are sold in more than 60 countries. Europe is its largest market, accounting for about 45% of sales, followed by the US, with about 30% of sales, and Asia, with 20%. The company operates three manufacturing plants in the US, in Colorado, Minnesota, and Texas. International plants are located in Denmark and Germany.

MOCON sells its products through both a direct sales force of 30 people and about 100 independent sales reps. Customers come from research laboratories, production departments, and quality control groups in the life science, medical, food, pharmaceutical, plastics, paper, electronics, and oil and gas industries.

MOCON has experienced steady growth for the past four years, and overall sales increased 14% in 2013 to $57 million after adding in the sales of its 2012 acquisition of Dansensor. Profits grew 70% to $3.4 million thanks to the revenue boost and a slight decrease in income tax. Its primary source of funds is cash flow from operating activities, and in 2013 the company had $3.6 million, a nice boost from the $500,000 it had in 2012.

The company also uses select acquisitions to drive growth. In 2012 MOCON paid $20 million for Denmark-based PBI-Dansensor, a maker of instruments used in Modified Atmosphere Packaging (MAP), which is used to extend the shelf life of foods, pharmaceuticals, and other perishable products. The purchase expanded MOCON's presence in Europe and in the growing MAP market with products that complement its package testing offerings. Dansensor makes gas mixers, headspace analyzers, and leak detection equipment, and most of its sales come from outside the US.

MOCON's stated business strategy is to capitalize on its product development, technological capabilities, and manufacturing processes. Its management team emphasizes product innovation, product performance, quality improvements, and cost reductions to keep it afloat. In addition, MOCON seeks growth opportunities through technological and product improvement, by acquiring and developing new products, by acquiring companies or new product lines, or by purchasing the rights to existing technologies.

MOCON founder Les Demorest was working as a research physicist for General Mills when he saw the need for moisture measurement instrumentation. He started the Demorest Instruments Company in 1963 to make such instruments. Demorest Instruments became Modern Controls, Inc. in 1966. The company went public in 1979 and changed its name to MOCON in 1999. Robert Demorest -- chairman, president, and CEO of MOCON since 2000 -- is the son of Les Demorest.
