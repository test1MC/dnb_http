Need an eight-story building fast? Not a problem for Butler Manufacturing, maker of pre-engineered buildings, structural systems, and roof and wall systems for nonresidential construction. Its leading brands include Butler and Varco Pruden. A subsidiary of Australia-based BlueScope Steel , Butler produces pre-engineered and custom-designed steel structures used in a range of projects, from offices to schools to shopping centers. Through its BUCON and Butler Heavy Structures units, the company provides general contracting services for large-scale projects. Butler also offers real estate development services. It distributes its products throughout North America.

Based in Missouri, Butler Manufacturing boasts production facilities across the US, Canada, and Mexico, regions where it distributes its products.

Butler Manufacturing sells its products through a network of full-service contractors and dealers located in North America and overseas. The company's products are used in a variety of markets, including commercial, community, industrial, and agricultural.

The company works to stay on top of trends through its Butler Builder Advisory Council, which meets regularly throughout the year to help steer development of the company's products, services, and programs and keeps a finger on the pulse of industry trends.

Butler Manufacturing's products are supplied by parent BlueScope's global supply chain and major manufacturing and engineering centers located in Asia and North America. BlueScope acquired Butler Manufacturing in 2004 for more than $200 million.

Founded in 1901, Butler Manufacturing started out building agricultural structures such as watering tanks and grain storage bins. The company soon diversified, creating a steel garage (its first metal product) in 1909 and even a biplane in the late 1920s. Butler's oil field equipment operations helped it survive the Depression, while its rigid-frame steel buildings catapulted the company's sales following WWII. With both agricultural and commercial construction operations, Butler fared well during the 1960s and 1970s, since the two markets never crashed at the same time during this period. After the agricultural market slumped in the early 1980s, the company started to divest its agricultural holdings and focus on commercial building operations. It acquired building product companies such as wall panel maker Inryco (1986).

After restructuring in the early 1990s, Butler benefited from a growing business both in the US and overseas. In 1997 Butler completely exited its original business when it sold its Grain Systems division. Having divested itself of its agricultural roots, the company bolstered its architectural products line by acquiring Modu-Line Windows and Rebco West.

The late 1990s brought further restructuring for Butler. In 1999 the company pulled out of Brazil in light of the economic crisis there, and it sold its Scotland-based subsidiary (spurred by the weak British pound) and moved its metal building operation to Hungary. Donald Pratt replaced the retiring Robert West as chairman that year. Company veteran John Holland assumed the president and CEO positions on the same date.

In 2001 a sluggish economy and winter construction slowdowns forced the company to cut about 170 jobs. The company sold its profitless European assets in early 2002. Butler planned to sell its Lester Building Systems business (engineered wood-frame buildings) to a management team headed by Lester division president John Hill in 2003, but the deal was terminated due to lack of consent from senior lenders.

The next year the company announced that it would merge with BlueScope Steel Limited , Australia's largest steel producer. Chicago-based Robertson-Ceco (pre-engineered metal buildings) made a rival takeover bid, but Butler rejected the offer and merged with BlueScope in April 2004.
