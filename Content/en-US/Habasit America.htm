Need a belt? Not for your trousers, but for your conveyor systems and other industrial machinery? Turn to Habasit America. The company makes power transmission belts and conveyor belts, along with related gears and motors, for material handling equipment in a variety of industries. Habasit America's products move automotive parts, beverages, electronic components, food, packaging, paper and tissue, pharmaceuticals, textiles, tires, tobacco, wood, and other commodities. Its conveyor belts also are used in airport baggage handling systems and supermarket check-out stands. Habasit America's Swiss parent company, Habasit Group Reinach, operates globally.

Habasit has more than 15 manufacturing facilities and 250 service centers around the world. It sells in some 70 countries.

Operations

The company's products included fabric-based conveyor belts, plastic modular belts and chains, power transmission belts, timing belts and gearmotors.

Habasit's clients are in the automotive, tobacco, food, printing and paper, textile, bottling and canning, packaging, and material handling sectors. It customers have included Genesys, M.I.R., CBT Solutions, Gemini Bakery Equipment, and Akron Belting.

The company was founded in 1946 by Fernand and Alice Habegger.

The firm later known as Summa Industries and Habasit America began in 1942 as Southern California Plastics Co., a manufacturer of plastic injection-molded products. In the 1960s it changed its name to Maxad. In 1974 Maxad bought (and renamed itself after) industrial machinery maker Morehouse Industries.

Morehouse began to diversify in the 1990s when it bought GST Industries (1991), formed Morehouse-COWLES (1992; sold 1996), and acquired KVP Systems (1993). In 1993 the company again changed its name, this time to Summa (Latin for "highest"), and bought the assets of Armenco Engineering, which it absorbed into Morehouse-COWLES in 1994.

Summa moved from building machinery to making plastic industrial components during the 1990s. It bought LexaLite, a company nearly three times its own size, in 1996. The next year Summa bought plastics maker Calnetics, including its Manchester Plastics, Ny-Glass Plastics, and Agricultural Products subsidiaries. Acquisitions continued in 1998 as Summa picked up former competitor Falcon Belting and consolidated it with its KVP Systems unit to form KVP Falcon Plastic Belting. That year Summa sold GST Industries, makers of non-plastic firefighting and military equipment, for about $2.7 million. Though GST was profitable, Summa believed the unit's growth prospects were unattractive.

In 1999 Summa bought Plastron Industries and Broadview Injection Molding, both privately held makers of thermoplastic parts used in wound electronic components such as relays and coils. The next year it added Yarbrough-Timco (molded plastic coils), Plastic Specialties (thermoplastic lenses), and the Ram Belts & Chains unit of Rainbow Industrial Products Corp. With its stock price languishing, in August 2001 Summa announced that it would seek potential buyers for the company. It retracted the announcement only one month later, citing uncertain business conditions. Summa acquired Pavaco Plastics' Genesta Manufacturing division in 2002; Genesta made extruded profiles and thermoformed components.

In 2005 the company sold its Plastron Industries (plastic winding cores found in small electric motors and transformers) segment.

In 2006 Habasit Holding AG bought Summa Industries for some $60 million; the company was renamed Habasit America. Habasit Holding, a privately held Swiss firm, also assumed around $21 million in Summa's debt.
