Exar seeks excellence in the exacting world of integrated circuits. The fabless semiconductor company's digital, analog, and mixed-signal integrated circuits (ICs) are used in networking equipment -- especially telecom infrastructure gear -- as well as in video and imaging devices, such as handheld electronics, set-top boxes, and DVRs. It also makes ICs and subsystems for the power management and datacom and storage markets, including storage optimization and network security processors. Customers include Alcatel Lucent , EMC , Huawei , Teradata , and ZTE . The company gets about 85% of its sales outside the US.

Exar outsources all of its fabrication and assembly, as well as the majority of testing operations to subcontractors in China, Indonesia, Malaysia, and Taiwan. The fabless manufacturing model allows it to focus on product design, development and support, and sales and marketing.

The company generates almost 50% of its revenue from its industrial and embedded systems segment, followed by 32% from the high-end consumer segment, and rounded out by the infrastructure segment with 19%.

Exar has international offices in Canada, China, France, Germany, Japan, Malaysia, South Korea, Taiwan, and the UK. Asia accounts for 60% of sales, the US accounts for 15% of sales, and the EMEA region (Europe, Middle East, and Africa) accounts for about 10%.

The company relies on a network of distributors, including Allied Electronics , Arrow Electronics , Digi-Key , Future Electronics , and Newark Corporation , to sell about half of its products. The other half is sold by a direct sales force.

Sales ran up about 30% in 2015 to $162 million for Exar. The acquisitions of Stretch (processor products) and iML (flat panel display products) helped boost revenue from industrial and embedded systems and high-end systems. But after two years in the black, a plunge took the company to a loss of about $45 million. The loss was attributed to higher operating costs from an impairment of intangibles and income taxes. Cash flow from operations also dove, from $851,000 positive to a negative $13.6 million.

Exar's acquisition of Integrated Memory Logic (iML) prompted the company to significantly restructure its operations in its fiscal 2015. Exar cut its headcount, which included staff reduction at its Hangzhou, China and Loveland, Colorado units. The result was the intangible assets impairment in 2015 that pushed the net loss deeper. Exar has re-oriented processes to reduce design time and increase the chance of success in the market. The company increased its research and development budget to $37 million in 2015 from $27 million in 2013.

Exar's $220 million acquisition of iML, provider of analog mixed-signal solutions for flat panel displays for TVs and tablets, buttresses Exar's intention to develop a large scale diversified analog mixed-signal business. Besides expanding Exar's portfolio, the deal expands its exposure in China, Korea, Taiwan, and Japan where most of iML's customers are.

The company also bought Stretch Inc., a provider of software configurable processors for video surveillance. The deal brings an end-to-end high-definition technology for digital and analog transmission of data from the camera to a recorder. Stretch's S6000 series and S7000 series products provide Exar a platform to integrate complex and high-value discrete components.
